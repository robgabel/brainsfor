# How Gary Vaynerchuk’s childhood in Edison helps him crush it in business

Chuck O'Donnell

Correspondent

Feb. 20, 2018, 12:00 p.m. ET

On those rare occasions when Gary Vaynerchuk finds a little free time, he has been known to roam Central Jersey in search of a good yard sale or flea market.

As his profile rises, however, it is becoming harder for him to retain his anonymity.

When you begin with modest means in Edison and build your family’s wine business into a $60 million juggernaut, launch one of the world’s hottest digital agencies, author a handful of books, become a sought-after public speaker and declare your intention to someday buy the New York Jets, it’s hard to not get recognized flipping through a box of CDs in someone’s driveway.

Even so, he was recently able to scour a garage sale in Kenilworth without any fanfare. He rooted through boxes of beat up sports gear and gently used baby clothes upon something stopped him cold.

“They were actually selling at the garage sale table my original wine book,” Vaynerchuk said. “I grabbed it and I said, ‘How much for this?’ (They said,) ‘A quarter.’ I said, ‘Ow! That hurts.’ They didn’t understand. I showed them my license. They thought it was the funniest thing.”

Who knows if he will one day find his latest book on the discount pile, but for now, it’s a national best-seller.

“Crushing It! How Great Entrepreneurs Build Their Business and Influence — And How You Can Too” is the magnetic mogul’s two-pronged approach to success for the aspiring entrepreneur.

The first part of the book orders the reader to “Get pumped.” It’s a one-stop shopping trip down the aisles of the human psyche in search of the right mental approach for business.

Vaynerchuk offers sage advice about how qualities such as passion and hard work are as important today as they were when he penned “Crush It!” in 2009. If you don’t believe it, the book offers vignettes of successful magnates and tastemakers and how they achieved success.

The second part of “Crushing It” is labeled “Create Your Pillar.” It’s a how-to guide on using Twitter, Musical.ly, YouTube and other social media and autdiocentric platforms to amplify your personal brand. It starts off with the edict that, no matter what kind of influencer you want to become, everyone should start by creating a Facebook business page.

Vaynerchuk realizes people might glean more information from one part of the book or the other, and that’s fine with him.

“I think it comes down to whatever gets somebody to do something,” said Vaynerchuk, who will be signing copies of “Crushing it” at the Wine Library in Springfield on Saturday, Feb. 24, at 11 a.m.

“I’m super pumped if it’s the inspiration and they get really rah-rahed out and they take the energy and bring it all the way home," he said. "Or, if they take a practical piece of advice where they’re like, ‘Oh, I can do that,’ and they then do it and it works and they build on it. I’m not romantic about what it gets someone to do something that changes their life. I’m romantic about just getting to that place.”

## Getting in business

The story of how Vaynerchuk began crushing has been well documented. The son of immigrants from Belarus, he went to work in the family business, Shoppers Discount Wine & Liquor in Springfield, when he was 14. He put sales on line. rechristened it the Wine Library, began a wine-related webcast and built it into a multimillion dollar business.

He launched VaynerMedia with his brother, A.J., in 2009, and began offering full-service digital strategies to Anheuser-Busch, Toyota, General Electric and other corporate giants.

In addition to his other companies and his investments, he has become a brand unto himself with an infectious enthusiasm and a rapid-fire public speaking rate that tops out at about 200 words per minute.

What has not been widely reported is how the root of all these lessons in life and business that he’s trying to impart in “Crushing It” can be traced back to his childhood home at 53 Teaberry Drive in the Dogwood Meadows development in Edison.

He says money was tight, so he took lemons and made lemonade — a lot of it. In fact, he set up a string of lemonade stands and ran them like franchises. And since this was the 1980s and he couldn’t take his own advice and use Snapchat to create buzz, he posted makeshift signs up and down Oak Tree Road and Tingley Lane to advertise his stands. Eventually, he would learn to hustle baseball cards and G.I. Joe actions figures to make a buck.

When asked if you could draw a line from that kid peddling Garbage Pail Kids cards to the angel investor he’s become today, his voice rises even higher.

“In a way you couldn’t imagine,” Vaynerchuk said. “Tingley Lane was where I did my lemonade stands that are super iconic at least as to how I relate my own self to myself," he said. "The JCC on Oak Tree Road was where I did my first baseball card show. John Adams Middle School is the place I got into the baseball card club and my infatuation with that and I learned how to do it.

"Clark, New Jersey, was where my dad’s first liquor store was and where I kind of went to the store on weekends and smelled the thoughts of a family business," he added. "Not only can it be traced to, but it has made such a greater impact on me than I even allude to.”

## Connecting with other immigrants

The 1980s were also a time when many families from India were settling in Edison. Vaynerchuk said he felt an instant connection, if not admiration, for his fellow immigrants who had come to these shores in search of opportunity.

“I associated incredibly well with them because they had a lot of grit and they were starting out with zero — not different than where my family was just five years earlier,” he said.

The Vaynerchuks eventually relocated and Gary would graduate from North Hunterdon High School, but the Edison environment and his own immigrant’s experience seem to have shaped Vaynerchuk to his core. It's like he's on this never-ending process of training his body and mind for success. There has never been an off switch, not even when he played basketball for years with AJ, who is 11 years his junior. Not once — not on the playground nor in the home on a Nerf basket — did Gary let his little brother win. AJ was 17 when he finally beat Gary.

Sounds cruel, but AJ says Gary was trying to teach him a lesson by not throwing him a token victory here or there.

“I think it teaches you that if you’re going to have success on your own merit, I think it does increase it,” A.J. said. “You should have seen how happy I was when I beat my brother in basketball for the first time. I think it makes you cherish accomplishments at a higher level because you truly earned it, you put in the work to get there and nothing really gets handed to you in life.”

No matter where in the world business takes him, Vaynerchuk has discovered you can go home again. Every few years, he will drive past his childhood home on Teaberry Drive, slow down and soak in the nostalgia. The memories of a sunlit childhood come rushing back.

He has come a long way from those days when he set up a miniature golf course in a friend’s backyard, but his past is never far behind.

“You have to understand, 1982 to 1989 is the fondest (period) of my life in a lot of ways,” Vaynerchuk said. “It’s when I became Americanized. It’s when I fell in love with the Jets. It’s when my family started making it in America. You know, Edison, New Jersey and then Springfield, New Jersey, where I cut my teeth at my dad’s liquor store and built Wine Library. That part of New Jersey means everything to me. And, a lot of towns in between, whether it’s Clark or Metuchen or Scotch Plains or Union. This is who I am, right? I played baseball in Colonia. This is who I am.”
