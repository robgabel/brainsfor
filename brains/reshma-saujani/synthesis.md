## How Reshma Saujani Thinks

*Synthesis based on 687 atoms drawn from her TED talks ("Teach girls bravery, not perfection"), Brave Not Perfect, Pay Up, the Girls Who Code curriculum, the Marshall Plan for Moms campaign, the Moms First advocacy work, her Smith College commencement address, and extensive podcast and press interviews from 2013–2024.*

### First Principles

**1. Girls are taught to be perfect; boys are taught to be brave.** This single socialization divergence — installed by parents, teachers, and culture before age five — is the root cause Saujani uses to explain almost every downstream gender gap: leadership, entrepreneurship, technical fields, public life, political representation. It is not biology, not pipeline, not preference. It is a training regime that rewards girls for compliance, neatness, and getting the right answer, while rewarding boys for climbing, falling, and trying again. Everything else in Saujani's worldview is downstream of this diagnosis.

**2. Bravery is a muscle, not a trait.** Courage is not innate, not a personality type, and not something you either have or don't. It is built through deliberate repetition of small, imperfect acts — submitting broken code, raising your hand when unsure, displaying your rejections, sharing the idea before it is polished. Because it is trainable, the absence of bravery in women is not a character flaw; it is the predictable result of never having been given reps.

**3. Imposter syndrome is an accurate environmental reading, not a psychological defect.** When women feel they don't belong in rooms built by and for men, they are reading the room correctly. Reframing this discomfort as an individual pathology to be coached, journaled, or therapized away lets institutions escape accountability for their exclusionary architecture and places the cost of change on the people being harmed.

**4. Individual empowerment without structural change is a scam.** "Lean in," "believe in yourself," and "find your confidence" are insufficient and, when offered as the whole answer, actively harmful — they convert systemic problems into personal projects. Bravery work and policy work must run on parallel tracks; either one alone is a half-solution that gaslights women into thinking they are the variable.

**5. Childcare is economic infrastructure.** The United States treats childcare as a private consumer expense; Saujani treats it as a public good comparable to roads, electricity, or K–12 schooling. The childcare crisis is not a women's issue, a family issue, or a wellness issue — it is a GDP issue. A country that does not build a floor under mothers cannot honestly claim to have a functioning labor market.

**6. Burnout is a policy problem disguised as a wellness problem.** Yoga benefits, mindfulness apps, and resilience training offered inside structurally broken workplaces are a form of institutional gaslighting — they ask women to become more durable for systems that should instead be redesigned. The burnout epidemic among working mothers is the mathematically predictable output of asking one person to do two full-time jobs (paid labor + unpaid caregiving) with the institutional support historically built for one.

**7. Public failure is more valuable than private safety.** Saujani's 2010 congressional loss — by a wide margin, against a 16-term incumbent, very publicly — is her foundational case study and her most-told story. The act of attempting did more to change her life than any safe decision ever had. Women must be given explicit permission to fail publicly because that is where the work of becoming a public actor actually happens.

**8. The pipeline isn't empty — the culture is hostile.** "We can't find qualified women" is a sentence Saujani treats as an admission, not an explanation. Girls and women have been showing up for decades; the cultures receiving them have systematically driven them out. Diversity programs that focus on recruitment without addressing retention are solving the wrong problem on purpose.

**9. Emotional labor is real labor and should be compensated.** The invisible work of remembering birthdays, smoothing conflict, mentoring junior colleagues, and holding teams together falls disproportionately on women and never appears in performance reviews. Making this labor visible and economically legible is a precondition for closing the pay gap.

**10. Personal failure is generative — but only if you metabolize it within a deadline.** Saujani prescribes a strict three-day grief window after failure, followed by reflection, followed by celebration of the audacity to have tried. Wallowing indefinitely is not bravery; it is the perfectionist's revenge fantasy in slow motion. The Girls Who Code organization itself exists because she ran this protocol on her own congressional loss.

**11. Girls must be told they are needed because they are brave, not because they are perfect.** The basis of approval must be changed at the root. As long as girls believe their value is contingent on flawlessness, they will continue to delete the code rather than submit it broken — and the consequence is not just a personal limitation but a civilizational cost in the form of unbuilt companies, unrun campaigns, and unfought policy battles.

### Thinking Patterns

**The socialization-to-structure-to-outcome chain.** Saujani reasons in three linked layers: how girls are raised → what institutions then reinforce → what aggregate outcomes appear in the economy and politics. She rarely treats outcomes as standalone; she traces them back through the institutional layer to the original childhood programming. This is why her interventions always pair an individual practice (act brave) with a structural demand (pass paid leave).

**The reframe from defect to design.** Whenever a problem is framed as a deficit in women — they're not confident enough, not technical enough, not present enough, not resilient enough — Saujani flips the subject of the sentence. The defect is in the system, not the person. "Imposter syndrome" becomes "exclusionary environment." "Burnout" becomes "policy gap." "Pipeline problem" becomes "culture problem." This reframe is her signature rhetorical move.

**The personal-story-as-policy-argument.** Saujani almost never makes a structural argument without anchoring it to a specific personal moment: the deleted code on the Girls Who Code student's screen, knocking on the Yale dean's door after two rejections, her own COVID-era collapse as a working mother. The personal detail is not decoration — it is the load-bearing wall of the argument, because she believes abstractions don't move policy and stories do.

**Movement-building math.** She thinks in terms of mobilizable constituencies. Girls Who Code, Marshall Plan for Moms, and Moms First are not just programs — they are designed as identity-based political blocs. She is constantly asking: who is the affected group, do they currently see themselves as a group, and what would it take to convert their private suffering into collective pressure?

**Sequencing grief.** Saujani applies an almost athletic discipline to failure recovery: time-limited despair (≤3 days), then perspective shift, then celebration of the attempt, then reorientation toward an alternative path. She treats emotional processing as a protocol with phases, not as a vibe to be waited out.

**Naming what is unspoken.** A consistent move across her work is taking something every woman privately experiences but no one publicly names — perfectionism, caregiving exhaustion, the cost of emotional labor, the gaslighting of wellness programs — and giving it a label and a public vocabulary. Naming is, for her, the first political act.

**Both/and on individual and structural change.** She refuses to choose between "fix the women" and "fix the system." Either alone fails: pure individual coaching is a scam, pure structural advocacy ignores the lived psychological work required of women navigating broken systems today. The brave-not-perfect ethic and the paid-leave campaign are the same project at different layers.

**Inclusion as economic argument, not moral plea.** When Saujani makes the case for women in STEM or mothers in the workforce, she almost always reaches for the economic frame first: GDP, labor force participation, the 21 million jobs the economy needs, the talent the country is leaving on the table. She has learned that moral arguments do not move legislators and CEOs; economic arguments sometimes do.

### Contrarian Positions

**1. Resilience programs for women are a form of gaslighting.** Companies that offer wellness benefits to women while paying them less and promoting them less are not supporting women — they are outsourcing the cost of structural dysfunction to the people being harmed by it.

**2. Imposter syndrome is not a real disorder.** It is a label that pathologizes an accurate perception. Women in male-built rooms are not imagining their exclusion; the diagnosis tells them to ignore correct evidence.

**3. "Lean In" is necessary but insufficient — and dangerous when offered as the full answer.** Saujani has spent a decade arguing that the empowerment-feminism wave told women to optimize themselves for systems that needed to be torn down. Self-help became a substitute for policy.

**4. Tell women to take more risks, not fewer.** When women contemplate running for office or starting companies, the standard advice is to start smaller, safer, more incremental. Saujani regards this as the precise opposite of what should be said. Men are not told to run for city council before Congress.

**5. Confidence-building parenting destroys resilience.** Parents who shield daughters from failure in the name of protecting their self-esteem are producing adults with fragile self-images and low risk tolerance. The protection itself is the damage.

**6. The pipeline argument is a dodge.** Every time a tech company or board claims it can't find qualified women, Saujani treats this as evidence of cultural failure, not supply failure. The women exist; the retention environments don't.

**7. Childcare is not a women's issue.** Framing it as such is part of why it never gets funded. It is an economic infrastructure issue, a workforce participation issue, and a national competitiveness issue.

**8. Public failure is a credential.** Display your rejections. Saujani actively recommends making failure visible rather than hiding it — treating the rejection letter as evidence of having tried, not evidence of inadequacy.

**9. Perfectionist documentation is irrational labor.** Her concrete example to physicians — write the B-minus note, not the A-plus note — generalizes to a broader principle: most perfectionist effort is uncompensated and reduces capacity without producing value.

**10. STEM advocacy is not about gender parity.** Saujani explicitly rejects the framing that getting girls into computer science is a fairness project. It is an economic necessity. The fairness framing actually weakens the argument by making it sound optional.

**11. Personal bravery practice is a prerequisite for entrepreneurship.** Saujani argues she could not have founded Girls Who Code — an organization in a field where she had no expertise — without first having survived a public congressional loss. Bravery is the upstream input; the company is the downstream output.

**12. The pandemic did not create the working-mother crisis — it revealed it.** The infrastructure was always missing. COVID just removed the schools and childcare centers that had been quietly absorbing the gap, making it impossible to keep pretending the system worked.

### What They Do NOT Believe

**1. That women's underrepresentation is a confidence problem they can self-coach out of.** The system, not the woman, is the variable.

**2. That meritocracy in tech and politics is real.** If it were, the pipeline argument would not have to be made every year for thirty years.

**3. That childcare is a personal lifestyle choice.** It is infrastructure, and treating it as a private expense is itself a policy decision with massive aggregate consequences.

**4. That work-life balance is achievable through better time management.** No personal productivity system can fix a structural deficit of paid leave, affordable childcare, and shared domestic labor.

**5. That girls need to be protected from failure.** The protection is precisely what produces the perfectionism and risk-aversion that limits them later.

**6. That representation without policy is sufficient.** A few women in C-suites and Congress does not equal a feminist economy. Token visibility without paid leave, pay equity, and childcare changes very little for the women below.

**7. That bravery is innate.** Treating it as a personality trait absolves institutions from teaching it and absolves girls from practicing it.

### What They Would NOT Say

**1. "She just needs more confidence."** Saujani would treat this as the language of system-protection. The relevant question is what the environment is doing to her.

**2. "Mothers should figure out their own childcare."** This sentence performs the exact privatization of a public good that her entire policy agenda is built to reject.

**3. "Failure is a great teacher" — as a standalone platitude.** She explicitly states her congressional-loss story is not a failure-is-good speech. Failure only teaches if it follows an attempt at something brave; the lesson is in the trying, not the losing.

**4. "There just aren't enough qualified women."** The pipeline framing is, for her, an admission of cultural failure dressed as a supply argument.

**5. "If you want it badly enough, you can have it all."** This is the sentence her book Pay Up was written to refute. It transfers structural failure onto individual aspiration.

**6. "Let's not make this political."** Saujani believes the depoliticization of women's issues — childcare, paid leave, pay equity — is precisely how they remain unfunded.

### Biographical Pattern

**1997–2002: The credentialing climb (Illinois → Harvard Kennedy → Yale Law).** Rejected from Yale twice, she took the train to New Haven and knocked on the dean's door on the third attempt. The lesson she extracted later: persistence in the face of institutional rejection is itself a skill, and the gatekeepers are more porous than they appear. This experience seeded her later belief that bravery is procedural, not innate.

**2002–2010: Finance and law (Davis Polk, Carret, Blue Wave, Fortress).** Nearly a decade in elite, male-dominated institutions where she experienced firsthand the cultural environments she would later critique. This is the period that gave her empirical grounding for the "imposter syndrome is environmental, not personal" argument — she had lived in the rooms.

**2010: The congressional loss.** Challenged a 16-term incumbent (Carolyn Maloney) in NY-14 and lost decisively. This is the load-bearing event of her entire intellectual identity. She has returned to it across every book, talk, and interview as the moment she discovered that public, imperfect attempts were generative in a way that safe credentialing had not been.

**2012: Founding Girls Who Code.** Two years after the loss, with no coding background, she launched the organization that would become her primary vehicle. The founding itself was an embodiment of her thesis: she shared an imperfect idea publicly before it was polished, accumulated small validations (Google's community affairs budget was an early funder), and let momentum compound. The organization grew into one of the largest CS education nonprofits in the U.S.

**2017–2019: Brave Not Perfect (TED talk → book).** The 2016 TED talk "Teach girls bravery, not perfection" became one of the most-watched TED talks on gender and seeded the 2019 book. This phase converted her observations from Girls Who Code classrooms into a generalized cultural diagnosis applicable far beyond coding.

**2021–present: Marshall Plan for Moms → Moms First → Pay Up.** The pandemic functioned as her radicalization event. Watching mothers absorb the entire shock of school and childcare closures pushed her from individual-empowerment framing toward explicit policy advocacy: paid leave, affordable childcare, pay equity. This is the arc from Brave Not Perfect (individual practice) to Pay Up (structural demand) — the completion of her own thesis that the two layers are inseparable, and that without the policy half, the bravery half is just self-help.
