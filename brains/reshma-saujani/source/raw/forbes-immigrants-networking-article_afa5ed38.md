# The Immigrant Connection

By [Megha Bahree](https://www.forbes.com/sites/meghabahree/ "https://www.forbes.com/sites/meghabahree/"),

Contributor.

Follow Author

Jan 21, 2010, 10:20am ESTJul 16, 2012, 05:29pm EDT

This article is more than10years old.

She calls it the city of aspiration, an apt term for a daughter of Indian immigrants who is the underdog in a Democratic primary race to take New York's 14th Congressional District away from nine-term incumbent Carolyn Maloney. On a recent afternoon at a lounge in a Manhattan apartment building, Reshma Saujani, 33, offered some fresh approaches to immigration policy, job creation and financial regulation--a subject she knows well as a former deputy general counsel at
Fortress Investment Group
. She got nods of assent from the dozen or so women present, most of them second-generation Indian-Americans.

"Some people in the party told me to wait my turn, that there were others in front of me in line," says Saujani. If she beats the odds, she would be among just a dozen current elected U.S. officials--mayors, state legislators and higher--of Indian parentage. The best known: Republican Bobby Jindal, Louisiana's governor, and Democrat Kamala Devi Harris, San Francisco's district attorney.

Some in Saujani's small audience will write checks for a campaign that has so far raised $400,000. This is just the first in a series of such meetings over wine and cheese and coffee to reach out to minorities, mothers and young people in a district with 30,000 Asians. Saujani is an experienced organizer, having worked as a fundraiser for Al Gore, John Kerry, Hillary Clinton and Barack Obama. She is tapping into those networks, other South Asian groups and an advisory council (Women for Reshma) of executives in the private and nonprofit sectors to spread the word, make introductions and solicit donations. Even as far afield as New Orleans, where the mother of a Bangladeshi friend carries a bio of Saujani in her purse. "She shows this to all her friends and tells them, 'We must support our girls,'" Saujani laughs.

What some would call grassroots organizing is a transplanted form of India's "aunty network." Aunties aren't necessarily related to the beneficiary; they are a community of supporters who spread news, offer emotional and financial support, and, of course, help arrange marriages. Over here aunty (and uncle) networks help launch careers. People of Indian descent are the most educated ethnic group in America, says Richard T. Herman, coauthor of _Immigrant, Inc._ (John Wiley & Sons, 2009): Nearly three in four who are 24 or older hold a four-year college degree, compared with a national average of 28%. They're also the wealthiest. The median household income for ethnic Indians was $91,200 in 2007, says the U.S. Census Bureau, compared with $50,300 for all households. Indians constitute a disproportionate share of U.S. engineers, technology workers, college professors and doctors. "America is warming up to this wave of talent," says Herman.

The group hug is pretty recent. Many Indian-Americans, particularly those in business, have had to rely on professional and social networks to turn their talents into success. For every Indra Nooyi (
PepsiCo
's chief) and Vikram Pandit (
Citigroup
's) there are thousands of Vivek Wadhwas. A decade ago he was having no luck funding his second company, Relativity Technologies, which sells software to help companies update it systems. After no venture capitalist in North Carolina returned his calls, he cold-called Vinod Khosla, the billionaire VC, who offered advice and introductions to, among others, Kanwal Rekhi. A founder of Indus Entrepreneurs, a nonprofit network known as tie, Rekhi urged Wadhwa to pitch to Greylock Partners in San Mateo, Calif. Suddenly Wadhwa heard back from North Carolina VCs. "\[Khosla and Rekhi\] didn't spend more than two hours on the phone with me," he recalls. "But because of their advice I could break the code." (Once a rising star that raised a total $24 million, Relativity was sold to a U.K. tech firm in late 2008 for $9.7 million.)

In 2005 Manish Chandra leaned on connections to launch Kaboodle.com, a site that mixes social networking with shopping. Chandra, an Indian Institute of Technology grad, contacted an IIT alumni group in the U.S., one of whose members worked at the Woodside Fund in Redwood Shores, Calif. He fine-tuned Chandra's pitch, led him to backers, helped recruit management and became an angel investor. Hobnobbing with tie, Chandra ran into the late Rajeev Motwani, a Stanford prof, IIT alum and mentor to
Google
's founders. Motwani invested in Kaboodle and made introductions at Ebay, Amazon, Google and other places. Kaboodle took off.
