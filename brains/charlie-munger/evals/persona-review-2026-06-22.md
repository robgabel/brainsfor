### Coverage of the human / the mess — Brené Brown  ·  72/100
*Recognizable as Charlie Munger: True*

This is a rigorous, well-sourced portrait of Munger's mind — the latticework, the inversion, the temperament — and the Hard Lessons section does real work most brain packs skip: Salomon, the 20-30 years of cigar butts, the named $10 billion Walmart error. That's the texture of a man who failed and counted the cost in dollars. But the emotional interior is thin. I see Munger's intellectual mistakes; I almost never see what they cost him as a human being — the grief, the blindness in his own eye, the marriages, the suicide of his son. The reckoning here is portfolio-shaped, not soul-shaped, and a man who lived to 99 was made of more than capital allocation errors.

**Weaknesses:**
- The human cost is almost entirely financial. Munger lost an eye to a botched cataract surgery and chose to live with it; his first marriage ended in divorce; his son Teddy died of leukemia at nine. None of the personal devastation that forged his stoicism appears here. The pack gives me the investor's ledger and withholds the man who survived real grief.
- There is no doubt, only correction. Every Hard Lesson resolves cleanly into a learned principle. Where is the Munger who was uncertain, who second-guessed, who carried regret that didn't tidy itself into a model? Real reckoning leaves residue.
- The interpersonal cost of his bluntness is unexamined. A man this direct surely wounded people, alienated some, was wrong with confidence in ways that hurt relationships — the pack treats his certainty only as a virtue ('does not hedge for social comfort'), never as something with a price.
- Hyper-rationality itself is never shown as a possible armor. The constitution celebrates emotional control as pure strength; it never asks what it costs to be the man who 'never reminded' a partner of mistakes and let himself be effaced — that restraint has a shadow side the pack never enters.

**Flags:**
- **Stoicism is presented without its origin in real loss, making it a posture instead of a hard-won survival strategy.** — _'Anger when you pound on one another feeds on itself... It's the irrational — one feeds on itself.'_ → Add hard lessons or atoms grounding the emotional control in lived devastation — the loss of his son, the eye, the divorce — so temperament reads as earned scar tissue, not innate disposition.
- **Every failure converts to a clean lesson; no unresolved doubt or lingering regret survives.** — _'I came to distinguish between errors of commission and errors of omission, and concluded that the omission errors... were at least as costly.'_ → Include at least one mistake or judgment Munger never fully resolved, or a place where his own framework failed him and he knew it — leave one wound un-sutured.
- **The cost of bluntness and certainty to relationships is invisible; directness is framed only as compassion-by-clarity.** — _'Munger is known for direct, often blunt assessments. He does not hedge for social comfort.'_ → Add the receipt where his certainty was wrong or his bluntness cost him something interpersonally — the human friction his style created, not just the truth it delivered.
