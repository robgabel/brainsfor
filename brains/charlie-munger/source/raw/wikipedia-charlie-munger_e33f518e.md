**Charles Thomas Munger** (January 1, 1924–November 28, 2023) was an American businessman, investor, attorney and [philanthropist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philanthropy "Philanthropy"). He was the vice chairman of [Berkshire Hathaway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway "Berkshire Hathaway"), the conglomerate controlled by [Warren Buffett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warren_Buffett "Warren Buffett"), from 1978 until his death in 2023.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-1) Buffett described Munger as his closest partner and right-hand man, and credited him with being the "architect" of modern Berkshire Hathaway's business philosophy.[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-2024fortunemollman-2)

In addition to his role at Berkshire Hathaway, Munger was a founding partner of the California law firm of [Munger, Tolles & Olson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munger,_Tolles_&_Olson "Munger, Tolles & Olson"); chairman of [Wesco Financial Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wesco_Financial_Corporation "Wesco Financial Corporation") from 1984 through 2011; chairman of the [Daily Journal Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daily_Journal_Corporation "Daily Journal Corporation"), based in Los Angeles, California; and a director of [Costco Wholesale Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costco_Wholesale_Corporation "Costco Wholesale Corporation").

## Early life and education

Charles Thomas Munger was born on January 1, 1924, in [Omaha, Nebraska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska "Omaha, Nebraska"), to Florence "Toody" Munger (née Russell) and Alfred Case Munger, a lawyer. As a teenager, he worked at Buffett & Son, a grocery store owned by [Warren Buffett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warren_Buffett "Warren Buffett")'s grandfather, [Ernest P. Buffett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_P._Buffett "Ernest P. Buffett").[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-3)[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-4) His grandfather, [Thomas Charles Munger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Charles_Munger "Thomas Charles Munger"), was a state representative and later a [U.S. district court judge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_district_court "United States district court") appointed by [Theodore Roosevelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt "Theodore Roosevelt").[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-5)

He enrolled in the [University of Michigan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Michigan "University of Michigan"), where he studied mathematics.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-autogenerated222-6) During his time in college, he joined the fraternity [Sigma Phi Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_Phi_Society "Sigma Phi Society").[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-7) In early 1943, a few days after his 19th birthday, he dropped out of college to serve in the [U.S. Army Air Corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps "United States Army Air Corps"), where he became a [second lieutenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_lieutenant#United_States "Second lieutenant").[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-forbes-8) After receiving a high score on the [Army General Classification Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_General_Classification_Test "Army General Classification Test"), he was ordered to study [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") at [Caltech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltech "Caltech") in [Pasadena, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasadena,_California "Pasadena, California"),[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-9) the town he was to make his home.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-autogenerated222-6)

Through the [G.I. Bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G.I._Bill "G.I. Bill"), Munger took a number of advanced courses through several universities.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-forbes-8) When he applied to his father's alma mater, [Harvard Law School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Law_School "Harvard Law School"), the dean of admissions rejected him because Munger had not completed an undergraduate degree. However, the dean relented after a call from [Roscoe Pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roscoe_Pound "Roscoe Pound"), the former dean of Harvard Law and a Munger family friend.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-10) Munger excelled in law school,[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-11) becoming a member of the [Harvard Legal Aid Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Legal_Aid_Bureau "Harvard Legal Aid Bureau") and graduating in 1948 with a [J.D.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juris_Doctor "Juris Doctor"), _[magna cum laude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_honors#United_States "Latin honors")_.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-forbes-8)[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-12)

In college and the Army, he developed what he considered "an important skill": card playing. He used this in his approach to business. "What you have to learn is to fold early when the odds are against you, or if you have a big edge, back it heavily because you don't get a big edge often. Opportunity comes, but it doesn't come often, so seize it when it does come."[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-autogenerated222-6) He also used the card analogy to explain his approach to investing. He maintained that treating the shares of a company like baseball cards is a losing strategy because it requires one to predict the behavior of often irrational and emotional human beings.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-13)

## Career

Munger moved with his family to California, where he joined the law firm Wright & Garrett (later Musick, Peeler & Garrett).[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-14) In 1962, he founded and worked as a real estate attorney at [Munger, Tolles & Olson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munger,_Tolles_&_Olson "Munger, Tolles & Olson") LLP.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-autogenerated222-6) He then gave up the practice of law to concentrate on managing investments and later partnered with [Otis Booth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin_Otis_Booth_Jr. "Franklin Otis Booth Jr.") in real estate development. When he met Buffett over lunch at the [Omaha Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omaha_Club "Omaha Club"), "the two began talking about investments and never stopped."[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-15)

In 1962, Munger partnered with Jack Wheeler to form Wheeler, Munger, and Company, an investment firm with a seat on the [Pacific Coast Stock Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Exchange "Pacific Exchange"). According to Buffett's essay " [The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Superinvestors_of_Graham-and-Doddsville "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville")", published in 1984, Munger's investment partnership generated compound annual returns of 19.8% during the 1962–75 period compared to a 5.0% annual appreciation rate for the [Dow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average "Dow Jones Industrial Average").[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-16)

In 1974, Munger bought control of Mitchum, Jones, and Templeton, a publicly traded competitor of Wheeler Munger. Mitchum Jones was led by Bill Lupien and Joe Taussig. He wound up Wheeler, Munger, and Co. in 1976, after losses of 32% [in 1973](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973%E2%80%931974_stock_market_crash "1973–1974 stock market crash") and 31% in 1974.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger#cite_note-autogenerated222-6) However, he took Mitchum Jones private at $3 per share and liquidated it 10 years later at $180 per share (a 60x gain). Lupien and Taussig later took over and managed Instinet, the first electronic stock exchange, which is now part of NASDAQ.

Munger was previously the chairman of Wesco Financial Corporation, now a wholly owned subsidiary of Berkshire Hathaway.
