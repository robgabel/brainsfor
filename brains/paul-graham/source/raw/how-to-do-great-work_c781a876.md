| ![How to Do Great Work](https://s.turbifycdn.com/aah/paulgraham/how-to-do-great-work-2.gif)<br>July 2023<br>If you collected lists of techniques for doing great work in a lot<br>of different fields, what would the intersection look like? I decided<br>to find out by making it.<br>Partly my goal was to create a guide that could be used by someone<br>working in any field. But I was also curious about the shape of the<br>intersection. And one thing this exercise shows is that it does<br>have a definite shape; it's not just a point labelled "work hard."<br>The following recipe assumes you're very ambitious.<br>The first step is to decide what to work on. The work you choose<br>needs to have three qualities: it has to be something you have a<br>natural aptitude for, that you have a deep interest in, and that<br>offers scope to do great work.<br>In practice you don't have to worry much about the third criterion.<br>Ambitious people are if anything already too conservative about it.<br>So all you need to do is find something you have an aptitude for<br>and great interest in.<br>\[ [1](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html#f1n)\]<br>That sounds straightforward, but it's often quite difficult. When<br>you're young you don't know what you're good at or what different<br>kinds of work are like. Some kinds of work you end up doing may not<br>even exist yet. So while some people know what they want to do at<br>14, most have to figure it out.<br>The way to figure out what to work on is by working. If you're not<br>sure what to work on, guess. But pick something and get going.<br>You'll probably guess wrong some of the time, but that's fine. It's<br>good to know about multiple things; some of the biggest discoveries<br>come from noticing connections between different fields.<br>Develop a habit of working on your own projects. Don't let "work"<br>mean something other people tell you to do. If you do manage to do<br>great work one day, it will probably be on a project of your own.<br>It may be within some bigger project, but you'll be driving your<br>part of it.<br>What should your projects be? Whatever seems to you excitingly<br>ambitious. As you grow older and your taste in projects evolves,<br>exciting and important will converge. At 7 it may seem excitingly<br>ambitious to build huge things out of Lego, then at 14 to teach<br>yourself calculus, till at 21 you're starting to explore unanswered<br>questions in physics. But always preserve excitingness.<br>There's a kind of excited curiosity that's both the engine and the<br>rudder of great work. It will not only drive you, but if you let<br>it have its way, will also show you what to work on.<br>What are you excessively curious about — curious to a degree that<br>would bore most other people? That's what you're looking for.<br>Once you've found something you're excessively interested in, the<br>next step is to learn enough about it to get you to one of the<br>frontiers of knowledge. Knowledge expands fractally, and from a<br>distance its edges look smooth, but once you learn enough to get<br>close to one, they turn out to be full of gaps.<br>The next step is to notice them. This takes some skill, because<br>your brain wants to ignore such gaps in order to make a simpler<br>model of the world. Many discoveries have come from asking questions<br>about things that everyone else took for granted. <br>\[ [2](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html#f2n)\]<br>If the answers seem strange, so much the better. Great work often<br>has a tincture of strangeness. You see this from painting to math.<br>It would be affected to try to manufacture it, but if it appears,<br>embrace it.<br>Boldly chase outlier ideas, even if other people aren't interested<br>in them — in fact, especially if they aren't. If you're excited<br>about some possibility that everyone else ignores, and you have<br>enough expertise to say precisely what they're all overlooking,<br>that's as good a bet as you'll find.<br>\[ [3](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html#f3n)\]<br>Four steps: choose a field, learn enough to get to the frontier,<br>notice gaps, explore promising ones. This is how practically everyone<br>who's done great work has done it, from painters to physicists.<br>Steps two and four will require hard work. It may not be possible<br>to prove that you have to work hard to do great things, but the<br>empirical evidence is on the scale of the evidence for mortality.<br>That's why it's essential to work on something you're deeply<br>interested in. Interest will drive you to work harder than mere<br>diligence ever could.<br>The three most powerful motives are curiosity, delight, and the<br>desire to do something impressive. Sometimes they converge, and<br>that combination is the most powerful of all.<br>The big prize is to discover a new fractal bud. You notice a crack<br>in the surface of knowledge, pry it open, and there's a whole world<br>inside.<br>Let's talk a little more about the complicated business of figuring<br>out what to work on. The main reason it's hard is that you can't<br>tell what most kinds of work are like except by doing them. Which<br>means the four steps overlap: you may have to work at something for<br>years before you know how much you like it or how good you are at<br>it. And in the meantime you're not doing, and thus not learning<br>about, most other kinds of work. So in the worst case you choose<br>late based on very incomplete information.<br>\[ [4](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html#f4n)\]<br>The nature of ambition exacerbates this problem. Ambition comes in<br>two forms, one that precedes interest in the subject and one that<br>grows out of it. Most people who do great work have a mix, and the<br>more you have of the former, the harder it will be to decide what<br>to do.<br>The educational systems in most countries pretend it's easy. They<br>expect you to commit to a field long before you could know what<br>it's really like. And as a result an ambitious person on an optimal<br>trajectory will often read to the system as an instance of breakage.<br>It would be better if they at least admitted it — if they admitted<br>that the system not only can't do much to help you figure out what<br>to work on, but is designed on the assumption that you'll somehow<br>magically guess as a teenager. They don't tell you, but I will:<br>when it comes to figuring out what to work on, you're on your own.<br>Some people get lucky and do guess correctly, but the rest will<br>find themselves scrambling diagonally across tracks laid down on<br>the assumption that everyone does.<br>What should you do if you're young and ambitious but don't know<br>what to work on? What you should _not_ do is drift along passively,<br>assuming the problem will solve itself. You need to take action.<br>But there is no systematic procedure you can follow. When you read<br>biographies of people who've done great work, it's remarkable how<br>much luck is involved. They discover what to work on as a result<br>of a chance meeting, or by reading a book they happen to pick up.<br>So you need to make yourself a big target for luck, and the way to<br>do that is to be curious. Try lots of things, meet lots of people,<br>read lots of books, ask lots of questions.<br>\[ [5](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html#f5n)\]<br>When in doubt, optimize for interestingness. Fields change as you<br>learn more about them. What mathematicians do, for example, is very<br>different from what you do in high school math classes. So you need<br>to give different types of work a chance to show you what they're<br>like. But a field should become _increasingly_ interesting as you<br>learn more about it. If it doesn't, it's probably not for you.<br>Don't worry if you find you're interested in different things than<br>other people. The stranger your tastes in interestingness, the<br>better. Strange tastes are often strong ones, and a strong taste<br>for work means you'll be productive. And you're more likely to find<br>new things if you're looking where few have looked before.<br>One sign that you're suited for some kind of work is when you like<br>even the parts that other people find tedious or frightening.<br>But fields aren't people; you don't owe them any loyalty. If in the<br>course of working on one thing you discover another that's more<br>exciting, don't be afraid to switch.<br>If you're making something for people, make sure it's something<br>they actually want. The best way to do this is to make something<br>you yourself want. Write the story you want to read; build the tool<br>you want to use. Since your friends probably have similar interests,<br>this will also get you your initial audience.<br>This _should_ follow from the excitingness rule. Obviously the most<br>exciting story to write will be the one you want to read. The reason<br>I mention this case explicitly is that so many people get it wrong.<br>Instead of making what they want, they try to make what some<br>imaginary, more sophisticated audience wants. And once you go down<br>that route, you're lost.<br>\[ [6](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html#f6n)\]<br>There are a lot of forces that will lead you astray when you're<br>trying to figure out what to work on. Pretentiousness, fashion,<br>fear, money, politics, other people's wishes, eminent frauds. But<br>if you stick to what you find genuinely interesting, you'll be proof<br>against all of them. If you're interested, you're not astray.<br>Following your interests may sound like a rather passive strategy,<br>but in practice it usually means following them past all sorts of<br>obstacles. You usually have to risk rejection and failure. So it<br>does take a good deal of boldness.<br>But while you need boldness, you don't usually need much planning.<br>In most cases the recipe for doing great work is simply: work hard<br>on excitingly ambitious projects, and something good will come of<br>it. Instead of making a plan and then executing it, you just try<br>to preserve certain invariants.<br>The trouble with planning is that it only works for achievements<br>you can describe in advance. You can win a gold medal or get rich<br>by deciding to as a child and then tenaciously pursuing that goal,<br>but you can't discover natural selection that way.<br>I think for most people who want to do great work, the right strategy<br>is not to plan too much. At each stage do whatever seems most<br>interesting and gives you the best options for the future. I call<br>this approach "staying upwind." This is how most people who've done<br>great work seem to have done it.<br>Even when you've found something exciting to work on, working on<br>it is not always straightforward. There will be times when some new<br>idea makes you leap out of bed in the morning and get straight to<br>work. But there will also be plenty of times when things aren't<br>like that.<br>You don't just put out your sail and get blown forward by inspiration.<br>There are headwinds and currents and hidden shoals.
