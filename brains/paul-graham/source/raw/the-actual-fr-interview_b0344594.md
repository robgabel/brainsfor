| ![The (Actual) FR Interview](https://s.turbifycdn.com/aah/paulgraham/the-actual-fr-interview-3.gif)<br>_(Found\|Read_<br>_[interviewed](http://gigaom.com/2008/05/03/the-fr-interview-y-combinators-paul-graham/) me about Y Combinator, but edited_<br>_the questions and answers so much that the sense was often changed._<br>_Here are my original answers. I've used their new questions_<br>_whenever possible, but kept the original ones when they'd changed_<br>_so much that my answer became mysterious.)_<br>**F\|R:** What is the mathematical function from which Y Combinator<br>takes its name, and why did you choose this?<br>**Graham:** It's a function that builds recursive functions without<br>them needing to have names. The Y Combinator is one of those things<br>that seems miraculous when you first encounter it. You wouldn't<br>necessarily have expected such a thing to be possible. We named<br>the company after it partly because we thought it was such a cool<br>concept, and partly as a secret signal to the kind of people we<br>hoped would apply.<br>**F\|R:** How quickly can you now tell whether a start-up will make it?<br>And what are the key characteristics that indicate potential success<br>to Y Combinator?<br>**Graham:** We can never tell for sure. No investor can. But we are<br>trying hard to get better at predicting.<br>I think the key quality is determination. The founders who do the<br>best are the type of people who just refuse to fail. Most startups<br>have at least one low point where any reasonable person would give<br>up. That bottleneck is the reason there are so few successful<br>startups. The only people who get through it are the ones who have<br>an unreasonable aversion to failing.<br>**F\|R:** Several copycat incubators have sprung up since Y Combinator<br>launched in 2005 (TechStars, Y Europe, Seedcamp, BoostPhase). Can<br>your model be replicated?<br>**Graham:** There are a few things they haven't copied correctly, but<br>really it's not our model that distinguishes us. It's the people<br>that make the difference—not just us, but the 250 or so founders<br>we've now funded. The amount of knowledge accumulated in all these<br>heads is remarkable.<br>**F\|R:** I read that when you call Y Combinator winners, the founders<br>have only five minutes to accept. ("If people turn us down," he<br>says, "as far as we're concerned they've failed an IQ test.") Have<br>startups turned you down? Are there any that have turned Y Combinator<br>down and still gone on to succeed with a liquidity event?<br>**Graham:** You're confusing two separate things. The reason people<br>are supposed to decide quickly whether or not to accept is that<br>they already know everything except the percent we'll ask for.<br>They've already seen the deal terms, and they already know as much<br>as they're going to know about YC before actually working with us.<br>So they should already know when we call what percentage they'd be<br>ok with. Since all they have to do is subtract one integer from<br>another, five minutes should be enough.<br>The "IQ test" quote refers not to how fast they have to decide, but<br>the amount of equity we usually ask for. In the median case it's<br>6%. If we take 6%, we have to improve a startup's outcome by 6.4%<br>for them to end up net ahead. That's a ridiculously low bar. So<br>the IQ test is whether they grasp that.<br>There was one startup that turned us down because they received an<br>acquisition offer during the weekend when we did interviews. It<br>was a pretty good offer. I'd have taken it in their position, and<br>they did. But other than that I don't know of anyone who turned<br>us down and went on to succeed. There have only been about three<br>others who turned us down.<br>**F\|R:** How did you determine the 12-week term of each Y Combinator<br>class? Why is two months too short, or six months too long to<br>"incubate" a startup?<br>**Graham:** We discovered it by accident. When we first started YC,<br>we began with a summer program. We were trying to learn how to be<br>investors, so we invited college students to come to Cambridge and<br>start startups instead of getting summer jobs.<br>Now we're looking for founders who consider the startup as a real<br>job, not just a summer one. But we kept the 3 month cycle because<br>it is a good length of time to build a version 1. Some startups<br>may not be able to launch in such a short time, but they should all<br>be able to build something impressive.<br>**F\|R:** Name one thing founders can do to increase their odds of being<br>selected by Y Combinator. Is Andreessen right that "the market"<br>matters more than the idea, the tech, and even the talent?<br>**Graham:** Get good cofounders. You can't change who you are, at least<br>not in a short time. And the idea doesn't matter to us as much as<br>the people. So the best thing any indvidual can do is find good<br>people to work with.<br>I think Marc may be right that market is the biggest determinant<br>in the outcome of successful startups. But that's not unrelated<br>to the qualities of the founders. Smart people will find big<br>markets. |
