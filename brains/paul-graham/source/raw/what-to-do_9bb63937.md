| ![What to Do](https://s.turbifycdn.com/aah/paulgraham/what-to-do-1.gif)<br>March 2025<br>What should one do? That may seem a strange question, but it's not<br>meaningless or unanswerable. It's the sort of question kids ask<br>before they learn not to ask big questions. I only came across it<br>myself in the process of investigating something else. But once I<br>did, I thought I should at least try to answer it.<br>So what _should_ one do? One should help people, and take care of<br>the world. Those two are obvious. But is there anything else? When<br>I ask that, the answer that pops up is _Make good new things_.<br>I can't prove that one should do this, any more than I can prove<br>that one should help people or take care of the world. We're talking<br>about first principles here. But I can explain why this principle<br>makes sense. The most impressive thing humans can do is to think.<br>It may be the most impressive thing that can be done. And the best<br>kind of thinking, or more precisely the best proof that one has<br>thought well, is to make good new things.<br>I mean new things in a very general sense. Newton's physics was a<br>good new thing. Indeed, the first version of this principle was to<br>have good new ideas. But that didn't seem general enough: it didn't<br>include making art or music, for example, except insofar as they<br>embody new ideas. And while they may embody new ideas, that's not<br>all they embody, unless you stretch the word "idea" so uselessly<br>thin that it includes everything that goes through your nervous<br>system.<br>Even for ideas that one has consciously, though, I prefer the<br>phrasing "make good new things." There are other ways to describe<br>the best kind of thinking. To make discoveries, for example, or to<br>understand something more deeply than others have. But how well do<br>you understand something if you can't make a model of it, or write<br>about it? Indeed, trying to express what you understand is not just<br>a way to prove that you understand it, but a way to understand it<br>better.<br>Another reason I like this phrasing is that it biases us toward<br>creation. It causes us to prefer the kind of ideas that are naturally<br>seen as making things rather than, say, making critical observations<br>about things other people have made. Those are ideas too, and<br>sometimes valuable ones, but it's easy to trick oneself into believing<br>they're more valuable than they are. Criticism seems sophisticated,<br>and making new things often seems awkward, especially at first; and<br>yet it's precisely those first steps that are most rare and valuable.<br>Is newness essential? I think so. Obviously it's essential in<br>science. If you copied a paper of someone else's and published it<br>as your own, it would seem not merely unimpressive but dishonest.<br>And it's similar in the arts. A copy of a good painting can be a<br>pleasing thing, but it's not impressive in the way the original<br>was. Which in turn implies it's not impressive to make the same<br>thing over and over, however well; you're just copying yourself.<br>Note though that we're talking about a different kind of should<br>with this principle. Taking care of people and the world are shoulds<br>in the sense that they're one's duty, but making good new things<br>is a should in the sense that this is how to live to one's full<br>potential. Historically most rules about how to live have been a<br>mix of both kinds of should, though usually with more of the former<br>than the latter. <br>\[ [1](https://www.paulgraham.com/do.html#f1n)\]<br>For most of history the question "What should one do?" got much the<br>same answer everywhere, whether you asked Cicero or Confucius. You<br>should be wise, brave, honest, temperate, and just, uphold tradition,<br>and serve the public interest. There was a long stretch where in<br>some parts of the world the answer became "Serve God," but in<br>practice it was still considered good to be wise, brave, honest,<br>temperate, and just, uphold tradition, and serve the public interest.<br>And indeed this recipe would have seemed right to most Victorians.<br>But there's nothing in it about taking care of the world or making<br>new things, and that's a bit worrying, because it seems like this<br>question should be a timeless one. The answer shouldn't change much.<br>I'm not too worried that the traditional answers don't mention<br>taking care of the world. Obviously people only started to care<br>about that once it became clear we could ruin it. But how can making<br>good new things be important if the traditional answers don't mention<br>it?<br>The traditional answers were answers to a slightly different question.<br>They were answers to the question of how to be, rather than what<br>to do. The audience didn't have a lot of choice about what to do.<br>The audience up till recent centuries was the landowning class,<br>which was also the political class. They weren't choosing between<br>doing physics and writing novels. Their work was foreordained:<br>manage their estates, participate in politics, fight when necessary.<br>It was ok to do certain other kinds of work in one's spare time,<br>but ideally one didn't have any. Cicero's _De Officiis_ is one of the<br>great classical answers to the question of how to live, and in it<br>he explicitly says that he wouldn't even be writing it if he hadn't<br>been excluded from public life by recent political upheavals.<br>\[ [2](https://www.paulgraham.com/do.html#f2n)\]<br>There were of course people doing what we would now call "original<br>work," and they were often admired for it, but they weren't seen<br>as models. Archimedes knew that he was the first to prove that a<br>sphere has 2/3 the volume of the smallest enclosing cylinder and<br>was very pleased about it. But you don't find ancient writers urging<br>their readers to emulate him. They regarded him more as a prodigy<br>than a model.<br>Now many more of us can follow Archimedes's example and devote most<br>of our attention to one kind of work. He turned out to be a model<br>after all, along with a collection of other people that his<br>contemporaries would have found it strange to treat as a distinct<br>group, because the vein of people making new things ran at right<br>angles to the social hierarchy.<br>What kinds of new things count? I'd rather leave that question to<br>the makers of them. It would be a risky business to try to define<br>any kind of threshold, because new kinds of work are often despised<br>at first. Raymond Chandler was writing literal pulp fiction, and<br>he's now recognized as one of the best writers of the twentieth<br>century. Indeed this pattern is so common that you can use it as a<br>recipe: if you're excited about some kind of work that's not<br>considered prestigious and you can explain what everyone else is<br>overlooking about it, then this is not merely a kind of work that's<br>ok to do, but one to seek out.<br>The other reason I wouldn't want to define any thresholds is that<br>we don't need them. The kind of people who make good new things <br>don't need rules to keep them honest.<br>So there's my guess at a set of principles to live by: take care<br>of people and the world, and make good new things. Different people<br>will do these to varying degrees. There will presumably be lots who<br>focus entirely on taking care of people. There will be a few who<br>focus mostly on making new things. But even if you're one of those,<br>you should at least make sure that the new things you make don't<br>net _harm_ people or the world. And if you go a step further and<br>try to make things that help them, you may find you're ahead on the<br>trade. You'll be more constrained in what you can make, but you'll<br>make it with more energy.<br>On the other hand, if you make something amazing, you'll often be<br>helping people or the world even if you didn't mean to. Newton was<br>driven by curiosity and ambition, not by any practical effect his<br>work might have, and yet the practical effect of his work has been<br>enormous. And this seems the rule rather than the exception. So<br>if you think you can make something amazing, you should probably<br>just go ahead and do it.<br>**Notes**<br>\[1\]<br>We could treat all three as the same kind of should by saying<br>that it's one's duty to live well — for example by saying, as some<br>Christians have, that it's one's duty to make the most of one's<br>God-given gifts. But this seems one of those casuistries people<br>invented to evade the stern requirements of religion: it was permissible to<br>spend time studying math instead of praying or performing acts of<br>charity because otherwise you were rejecting a gift God had given<br>you. A useful casuistry no doubt, but we don't need it.<br>We could also combine the first two principles, since people are<br>part of the world. Why should our species get special treatment?<br>I won't try to justify this choice, but I'm skeptical that anyone<br>who claims to think differently actually lives according to their<br>principles.<br>\[2\]<br>Confucius was also excluded from public life after ending up<br>on the losing end of a power struggle, and presumably he too would<br>not be so famous now if it hadn't been for this long stretch of<br>enforced leisure.<br>**Thanks** to Trevor Blackwell, Jessica <br>Livingston, and Robert Morris for reading drafts of this. |
