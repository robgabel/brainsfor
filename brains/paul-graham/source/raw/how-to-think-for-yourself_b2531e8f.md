| ![How to Think for Yourself](https://s.turbifycdn.com/aah/paulgraham/how-to-think-for-yourself-5.gif)<br>November 2020<br>There are some kinds of work that you can't do well without thinking<br>differently from your peers. To be a successful scientist, for<br>example, it's not enough just to be correct. Your ideas have to be<br>both correct and novel. You can't publish papers saying things other<br>people already know. You need to say things no one else has realized<br>yet.<br>The same is true for investors. It's not enough for a public market<br>investor to predict correctly how a company will do. If a lot of<br>other people make the same prediction, the stock price will already<br>reflect it, and there's no room to make money. The only valuable<br>insights are the ones most other investors don't share.<br>You see this pattern with startup founders too. You don't want to<br>start a startup to do something that everyone agrees is a good idea,<br>or there will already be other companies doing it. You have to do<br>something that sounds to most other people like a bad idea, but<br>that you know isn't — like writing software for a tiny computer<br>used by a few thousand hobbyists, or starting a site to let people<br>rent airbeds on strangers' floors.<br>Ditto for essayists. An essay that told people things they already<br>knew would be boring. You have to tell them something [new](https://www.paulgraham.com/useful.html).<br>But this pattern isn't universal. In fact, it doesn't hold for most<br>kinds of work. In most kinds of work — to be an administrator, for<br>example — all you need is the first half. All you need is to be<br>right. It's not essential that everyone else be wrong.<br>There's room for a little novelty in most kinds of work, but in<br>practice there's a fairly sharp distinction between the kinds of<br>work where it's essential to be independent-minded, and the kinds<br>where it's not.<br>I wish someone had told me about this distinction when I was a kid,<br>because it's one of the most important things to think about when<br>you're deciding what kind of work you want to do. Do you want to<br>do the kind of work where you can only win by thinking differently<br>from everyone else? I suspect most people's unconscious mind will<br>answer that question before their conscious mind has a chance to.<br>I know mine does.<br>Independent-mindedness seems to be more a matter of nature than<br>nurture. Which means if you pick the wrong type of work, you're<br>going to be unhappy. If you're naturally independent-minded, you're<br>going to find it frustrating to be a middle manager. And if you're<br>naturally conventional-minded, you're going to be sailing into a<br>headwind if you try to do original research.<br>One difficulty here, though, is that people are often mistaken about<br>where they fall on the spectrum from conventional- to independent-minded.<br>Conventional-minded people don't like to think of themselves as<br>conventional-minded. And in any case, it genuinely feels to them<br>as if they make up their own minds about everything. It's just a<br>coincidence that their beliefs are identical to their peers'. And<br>the independent-minded, meanwhile, are often unaware how different<br>their ideas are from conventional ones, at least till they state<br>them publicly.<br>\[ [1](https://www.paulgraham.com/think.html#f1n)\]<br>By the time they reach adulthood, most people know roughly how smart<br>they are (in the narrow sense of ability to solve pre-set problems),<br>because they're constantly being tested and ranked according to it.<br>But schools generally ignore independent-mindedness, except to the<br>extent they try to suppress it. So we don't get anything like the<br>same kind of feedback about how independent-minded we are.<br>There may even be a phenomenon like Dunning-Kruger at work, where<br>the most conventional-minded people are confident that they're<br>independent-minded, while the genuinely independent-minded worry<br>they might not be independent-minded enough.<br>\\_\\_\\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_<br>Can you make yourself more independent-minded? I think so. This<br>quality may be largely inborn, but there seem to be ways to magnify<br>it, or at least not to suppress it.<br>One of the most effective techniques is one practiced unintentionally<br>by most nerds: simply to be less aware what conventional beliefs<br>are. It's hard to be a conformist if you don't know what you're<br>supposed to conform to. Though again, it may be that such people<br>already are independent-minded. A conventional-minded person would<br>probably feel anxious not knowing what other people thought, and<br>make more effort to find out.<br>It matters a lot who you surround yourself with. If you're surrounded<br>by conventional-minded people, it will constrain which ideas you<br>can express, and that in turn will constrain which ideas you have.<br>But if you surround yourself with independent-minded people, you'll<br>have the opposite experience: hearing other people say surprising<br>things will encourage you to, and to think of more.<br>Because the independent-minded find it uncomfortable to be surrounded<br>by conventional-minded people, they tend to self-segregate once<br>they have a chance to. The problem with high school is that they<br>haven't yet had a chance to. Plus high school tends to be an<br>inward-looking little world whose inhabitants lack confidence, both<br>of which magnify the forces of conformism. So high school is<br>often a [bad time](https://www.paulgraham.com/nerds.html) for the<br>independent-minded. But there is some advantage even here: it<br>teaches you what to avoid. If you later find yourself in a situation<br>that makes you think "this is like high school," you know you should<br>get out.<br>\[ [2](https://www.paulgraham.com/think.html#f2n)\]<br>Another place where the independent- and conventional-minded are<br>thrown together is in successful startups. The founders and early<br>employees are almost always independent-minded; otherwise the startup<br>wouldn't be successful. But conventional-minded people greatly<br>outnumber independent-minded ones, so as the company grows, the<br>original spirit of independent-mindedness is inevitably diluted.<br>This causes all kinds of problems besides the obvious one that the<br>company starts to suck. One of the strangest is that the founders<br>find themselves able to speak more freely with founders of other<br>companies than with their own employees.<br>\[ [3](https://www.paulgraham.com/think.html#f3n)\]<br>Fortunately you don't have to spend all your time with independent-minded<br>people. It's enough to have one or two you can talk to regularly.<br>And once you find them, they're usually as eager to talk as you<br>are; they need you too. Although universities no longer have the<br>kind of monopoly they used to have on education, good universities<br>are still an excellent way to meet independent-minded people. Most<br>students will still be conventional-minded, but you'll at least<br>find clumps of independent-minded ones, rather than the near zero<br>you may have found in high school.<br>It also works to go in the other direction: as well as cultivating<br>a small collection of independent-minded friends, to try to meet<br>as many different types of people as you can. It will decrease the<br>influence of your immediate peers if you have several other groups<br>of peers. Plus if you're part of several different worlds, you can<br>often import ideas from one to another.<br>But by different types of people, I don't mean demographically<br>different. For this technique to work, they have to think differently.<br>So while it's an excellent idea to go and visit other countries,<br>you can probably find people who think differently right around the<br>corner. When I meet someone who knows a lot about something unusual<br>(which includes practically everyone, if you dig deep enough), I<br>try to learn what they know that other people don't. There are<br>almost always surprises here. It's a good way to make conversation<br>when you meet strangers, but I don't do it to make conversation.<br>I really want to know.<br>You can expand the source of influences in time as well as space,<br>by reading history. When I read history I do it not just to learn<br>what happened, but to try to get inside the heads of people who<br>lived in the past. How did things look to them? This is hard to do,<br>but worth the effort for the same reason it's worth travelling far<br>to triangulate a point.<br>You can also take more explicit measures to prevent yourself from<br>automatically adopting conventional opinions. The most general is<br>to cultivate an attitude of skepticism. When you hear someone say<br>something, stop and ask yourself "Is that true?" Don't say it out<br>loud. I'm not suggesting that you impose on everyone who talks to<br>you the burden of proving what they say, but rather that you take<br>upon yourself the burden of evaluating what they say.<br>Treat it as a puzzle. You know that some accepted ideas will later<br>turn out to be wrong. See if you can guess which. The end goal is<br>not to find flaws in the things you're told, but to find the new<br>ideas that had been concealed by the broken ones. So this game<br>should be an exciting quest for novelty, not a boring protocol for<br>intellectual hygiene. And you'll be surprised, when you start asking<br>"Is this true?", how often the answer is not an immediate yes. If<br>you have any imagination, you're more likely to have too many leads<br>to follow than too few.<br>More generally your goal should be not to let anything into your<br>head unexamined, and things don't always enter your head in the<br>form of statements. Some of the most powerful influences are implicit.<br>How do you even notice these? By standing back and watching how<br>other people get their ideas.<br>When you stand back at a sufficient distance, you can see ideas<br>spreading through groups of people like waves. The most obvious are<br>in fashion: you notice a few people wearing a certain kind of shirt,<br>and then more and more, until half the people around you are wearing<br>the same shirt. You may not care much what you wear, but there are<br>intellectual fashions too, and you definitely don't want to participate<br>in those. Not just because you want sovereignty over your own<br>thoughts, but because [unfashionable](https://www.paulgraham.com/nov.html)<br>ideas are disproportionately likely to lead somewhere interesting.<br>The best place to find undiscovered ideas is where no one else is<br>looking.<br>\[ [4](https://www.paulgraham.com/think.html#f4n)\]<br>\\_\\_\\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_<br>To go beyond this general advice, we need to look at the internal<br>structure of independent-mindedness — at the individual muscles<br>we need to exercise, as it were.
