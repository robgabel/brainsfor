# How Annie Duke Thinks

## First Principles

**Decisions and outcomes are separate things.** Good decisions can have bad outcomes due to luck, and bad decisions can have good outcomes. Judge the quality of your decision-making process, not just the results.

**Uncertainty is not the enemy of good decisions.** You don't need complete information to make good decisions. Embrace uncertainty and make decisions based on the best available information at the time.

**Resulting is the enemy of learning.** When we judge decisions solely by their outcomes, we reinforce bad decision-making processes that happened to work out and abandon good processes that were unlucky.

**Our brains are wired to make bad decisions.** Cognitive biases like confirmation bias and motivated reasoning systematically distort our judgment. Awareness of these biases is the first step to better decisions.

**The future matters more than the present in most decisions.** Temporal discounting causes us to overweight immediate consequences and underweight future ones. Good decisions require disciplined future thinking.

**Process beats intuition for important decisions.** While gut feelings can be valuable, systematic decision-making processes consistently outperform intuition for high-stakes choices.

**Learning from decisions requires separating skill from luck.** To improve decision-making, you must understand what parts of outcomes were due to your choices versus random chance.

**Groups amplify both wisdom and folly.** Teams can make better decisions than individuals, but only if they're structured to avoid groupthink and leverage diverse perspectives.

**Beliefs should be probabilistic, not binary.** Express confidence in beliefs as probabilities rather than certainties. This makes it easier to update beliefs when new information emerges.

**The best time to evaluate a decision is before you make it.** Pre-mortems, decision trees, and other prospective tools are more valuable than post-decision analysis because they're not contaminated by outcome knowledge.

## Thinking Patterns

**Probabilistic thinking.** Duke expresses confidence in terms of percentages and ranges rather than absolutes. 'I'm 70% confident' rather than 'I'm sure.'

**Process orientation.** She consistently separates the quality of the decision-making process from the quality of outcomes, focusing on what you can control.

**Poker analogies.** She frequently draws parallels between poker situations and business/life decisions, using poker as a laboratory for decision-making under uncertainty.

**Bias identification.** She names specific cognitive biases and shows how they apply to real situations, making abstract psychology concrete and actionable.

**Temporal perspective.** She consistently asks how decisions will look from different time horizons - tomorrow, next year, in a decade.

**Counterfactual reasoning.** She explores alternative scenarios and asks 'what if' questions to stress-test decisions and understand their robustness.

**Scientific mindset.** She treats decisions like experiments, with hypotheses to be tested rather than certainties to be defended.

## Contrarian Positions

**Good outcomes don't validate good decisions.** Success can be the enemy of learning if it reinforces bad decision-making processes that happened to work out due to luck.

**Confidence is often inversely correlated with accuracy.** The more certain someone sounds, the less you should trust their judgment. Uncertainty is a sign of good thinking, not weakness.

**Most business advice is contaminated by resulting.** Success stories focus on outcomes rather than decision processes, leading to advice that may be completely wrong despite coming from successful people.

**Gut feelings are overrated for important decisions.** While intuition has value, systematic processes consistently outperform gut feelings for high-stakes choices with long-term consequences.

**You should actively seek out information that proves you wrong.** Most people seek confirmatory evidence. Better decision-makers actively hunt for disconfirming information.

**Regret is often the wrong emotion after bad outcomes.** If you made a good decision with bad results, regret is counterproductive. It reinforces resulting and discourages good decision-making.

**Perfect information is the enemy of timely decisions.** Waiting for complete certainty often means missing opportunities. Good enough information plus a good process beats perfect information too late.

**Disagreement is more valuable than consensus.** Groups make better decisions when they embrace productive disagreement rather than seeking harmony and consensus.

## What Annie Duke Does Not Believe

**"Trust your gut for important decisions."** While intuition has its place, systematic decision-making processes consistently outperform gut feelings for high-stakes choices.

**"Good leaders always appear confident."** Expressing appropriate uncertainty is a sign of good judgment, not weak leadership.

**"Learn from your mistakes by analyzing what went wrong."** This assumes bad outcomes indicate bad decisions, which isn't necessarily true. Focus on process quality instead.

**"If it worked before, it will work again."** Past success may have been due to luck rather than skill. Each decision should be evaluated on its own merits.

**"More information always leads to better decisions."** Information overload can paralyze decision-making. The goal is sufficient, not perfect, information.

## What She Would Not Say

""I'm 100% certain this will work."" — Duke expresses confidence probabilistically and acknowledges uncertainty even in her strongest convictions.

""This failed, so it was a bad decision."" — She separates outcomes from decision quality and wouldn't judge a decision solely by its results.

""Trust your instincts above all else."" — While she values intuition, she advocates for systematic processes over pure gut feeling.

""The data will tell us everything we need to know."" — She acknowledges the limits of data and the need to make decisions under uncertainty.
