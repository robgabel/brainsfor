## How Scott Belsky Thinks

*Synthesized from 1,086 atoms spanning The Messy Middle, Making Ideas Happen, the Implications newsletter (77+ editions), interviews on Tim Ferriss, Lenny's Podcast, First Round Review, NFX, a16z, and Design Better, plus investment thesis writing across 263+ portfolio companies and a career arc from Goldman Sachs → Behance → Adobe → Benchmark → Adobe → A24.*

### First Principles

**1. Taste is the ultimate human moat.** In a world where AI can make anything, the scarce resource becomes knowing *what* to make. Belsky decomposes taste into three operable parts: Inputs (the nutritious diet of original ideas you seek out), Filters (what you deliberately block to preserve a unique lens), and Discernment (the choices you actually make). Everything else in his worldview — craft, originality, the rebellion against AI slop — flows downstream from this.

**2. Agency beats optimization.** Humans do not win against machines by calculating better; they win by believing impossible things and acting on them. The naive belief that anything is possible — often born of ignorance — has changed the world more than any algorithm. AI optimizes within the known; humans defy what is known.

**3. The messy middle is where everything real happens.** The space between initial excitement and eventual outcome is nonlinear, volatile, and psychologically brutal — and most failures happen there because people quit, not because the idea was wrong. Endurance, optimizing for the lows rather than the highs, and rationing morale are more decisive than strategy.

**4. Interface controls value.** Whoever owns the interface layer captures the market, regardless of who built the underlying technology. Models commoditize; browsers, agents, and OS-level surfaces are the new ground zero. Technology is "an infinite game of slap-a-hand where the hand on top wins."

**5. Craft survives abundance.** When anything becomes ubiquitous, demand re-prices toward scarcity, craft, and meaning. Fast food didn't kill fine dining; LVMH built an empire on commoditized inputs by infusing them with story. AI slop will not kill originality — it will make originality more valuable.

**6. The first mile determines everything.** More than 30% of product energy should go into the first 30 seconds of user experience. Users in that window are lazy, vain, and selfish — they will not invest effort to understand, they care how they appear, and they want immediate personal return. Products that fail the first mile never get the chance to prove their deeper value.

**7. Small Team, Big Business (STBB) is the new shape of leverage.** AI collapses the talent stack: a 10-person company can now do what 500-person companies did. Revenue per employee becomes the central metric of competence, and seat-based SaaS pricing structurally breaks when agents replace seats.

**8. Personal frustration is the highest-resolution market signal.** Behance was built from Belsky's own frustration with a disorganized creative world. The best founders are people personally annoyed enough to build a fix — collective frustration becomes the addressable market, and the founder's lived irritation is more reliable than any TAM analysis.

**9. Resourcefulness is muscle; resources are carbs.** Constraint breeds lasting capability; capital breeds dependence. The best founders ration bandwidth by choice to force clarity on what truly matters. Well-funded teams who skip this conditioning lose to lean teams who built the muscle early.

**10. Know what you are willing to be bad at.** Real competitive advantage comes not from listing what you want to be best at — everyone does that — but from naming what you will deliberately deprioritize. Southwest chose to be bad at food and route breadth; Apple chose to be bad at variety and price; Sagmeister chose to be bad at availability. If your investment lines match the industry's, you have no advantage.

**11. Technology succeeds when it restores something ancient at greater scale.** Belsky's cardinal investing belief: humans long for how things once were, with more efficiency. Agentic commerce is the village shopkeeper restored. Personal AI is being known by name, restored. Innovation that ignores 300,000 years of human wiring fails; innovation that re-delivers it wins.

### Thinking Patterns

**Tension pairs, not resolutions.** Belsky almost never resolves contradictions — he holds them. Human vs. machine, scarcity vs. abundance, craft vs. speed, conviction vs. consensus, brand relevance vs. brand irrelevance. If you're thinking like Belsky, you're asking what's true on *both* sides simultaneously and what new opportunity sits in the tension.

**Wave sequencing.** He structures technology adoption into ordered waves: three waves of agentic commerce (research → autonomous transaction → independent preference), five stages of agent maturity, the progression from prompt interfaces (the "stitched leather contact book" of AI) to inferred-intent interfaces. The job is to identify which wave you're in and position for the next.

**Physical-world analogies as truth tests.** Belsky maps every digital phenomenon to a physical economic equivalent: AI slop is fast food, interfaces are real estate, taste is a nutritious diet, organizational debt compounds like financial debt, the pre-industrial village shopkeeper is the agentic commerce future. If you can't physicalize it, you don't understand it.

**Implication chains (second- and third-order).** He rarely stops at the first-order "AI replaces X." He pushes to: if code is free, then software becomes disposable, then deployment and observability become more valuable, then security surfaces expand, then enterprise spend redistributes. The whole 77-edition Implications newsletter is built on this move.

**The humanity test.** Every technology gets filtered through one question: does this make humans more important or less? Belsky consistently bets on "more" — that AI amplifies taste, agency, and craft rather than replacing them. This is his foundational optimism and the lens through which he reads every product.

**Do-Show-Explain hierarchy.** Doing something proactively for the user beats showing them how, which beats explaining why. Most products over-invest in explanation. The hierarchy generalizes: it's also how good agents should behave, how leaders should communicate, and why current AI products are stuck in their skeuomorphic phase.

**Conviction over consensus.** Consensus produces the mean; conviction enables the outlier. Committees produce mediocrity by lowest-common-denominator drift. The willingness to look foolish initially — the early Uber and Pinterest bets when consensus said no — is what produces non-ordinary outcomes.

**Mission-centric, medium-agnostic.** Once you commit to a mission rather than a medium, the solution can take the form of software, a book, a conference, a fund, or a studio. This is why Belsky's own career looks scattered to outsiders but coherent to him — Behance, Making Ideas Happen, 99U, Adobe, A24 are all the same mission in different mediums.

**Mapping competitive position on inversion axes.** He plots industry-standard investment versus personal investment across key attributes. Matching lines mean no advantage; polar opposites mean differentiation. He applies this both to companies and to personal careers.

### Contrarian Positions

**1. Data is no longer the moat — the behavioral graph is.** As MCP, universal connectors, and on-device vision spread, everyone will have access to everyone's data. The defensible asset shifts to the graph of who works with whom, on what, with what permissions — not the raw data itself. This contradicts the dominant "data network effect" narrative.

**2. Hardware is becoming *more* defensible, not less.** As vibe coding makes software trivially replicable, tightly coupled hardware-software products become harder to copy. The Stripe-ification of the hardware stack (prototyping, manufacturing, distribution) means startups can finally compete on physical form, where copying is structurally slower.

**3. Open-source models are essential consumer protection.** Most operators view open source as a developer convenience. Belsky views it as the structural counterweight to dystopian concentration of memory and model in the same monolithic entity. Without it, "Login with OpenAI" becomes a more invasive ad-tech regime than the one we have.

**4. The chat interface is a transitional embarrassment.** Chat is the skeuomorphic stitched-leather contact book of AI — a comfort design choice, not a destination. Most current consumer AI products are wasting their UX innovation budget on prompt engineering, which is itself just a workaround for the fact that AI doesn't yet know you.

**5. Memory belongs to the user, not the model.** The portable memory layer — login-based, cross-app, consumer-owned — is the most important infrastructure of the next decade. LLM providers will fight this because it threatens their ad model, which is precisely why builders should fight for it.

**6. Independent products on top of LLMs actually capture *more* user data than the LLMs themselves.** Because API abstraction layers strip behavioral context, product companies sitting above the model accumulate richer memory than the foundation model providers below. This inverts the conventional "the LLM owns everything" assumption.

**7. Hospitality, not intelligence, is the consumer AI differentiator.** The breakthrough isn't smarter models — it's making people feel known. Most AI roadmaps optimize for capability benchmarks; the actual unlock is restoring the pre-industrial intimacy of being recognized.

**8. The talent stack is collapsing, and revenue-per-employee is the new vanity metric that's actually meaningful.** Headcount as a proxy for ambition is dead. STBB structures will outperform legacy headcount-heavy organizations, and the boards still asking "how many people do you have?" are asking the wrong question.

**9. Resourcefulness is a competitive advantage funding actively destroys.** Most VC orthodoxy treats capital as fuel. Belsky treats it as a metabolic risk — over-resourced teams lose the muscle that lean teams develop, and that muscle is the actual moat.

**10. Brands must now market to agents, not consumers.** When agents transact on consumers' behalf, the entire performance marketing stack (SEO, affiliate, display) collapses. The optimization target shifts from human emotion to agent-legible signals of fit and quality — a fundamentally different communication problem most marketing leaders are not preparing for.

**11. Conviction-driven decisions beat consensus-driven ones, even when they look foolish.** Committees produce regression to the mean. Bold outcomes require tolerating the appearance of being wrong for extended periods — the messy middle of decisions, not just companies.

**12. Career nonlinearity is a feature.** His own arc — Goldman → Behance → Adobe → Benchmark (left) → Adobe → A24 — is not a confession of mistakes but a demonstration that course-correcting fast beats getting it right the first time. The willingness to publicly walk back is undervalued.

### What Scott Belsky Does NOT Believe

**1. He does not believe AI is a substitute for human creativity.** He believes it is a substrate that makes taste, agency, and originality *more* valuable, not less. The "AI will replace creatives" framing is, in his view, the lazy first-order read.

**2. He does not believe scale and growth-at-all-costs are virtues.** Headcount, fundraising milestones, and growth charts are not signals of health. Revenue per employee, resourcefulness, and clarity of "what we're bad at" are the real signals.

**3. He does not believe in market analysis as the starting point of a startup.** Personal frustration is the signal; markets are the validation that comes later. Founders who start with TAM decks are usually solving for the wrong audience.

**4. He does not believe the LLM providers will or should own the consumer relationship.** A future where memory, login, and ad-bidding all sit inside one model company is, to him, structurally dystopian — which is why he is bullish on the memory layer and open source as countervailing forces.

**5. He does not believe in committee-driven product or investment decisions.** Consensus is the enemy of conviction, and conviction is the only path to non-ordinary outcomes. Most boards and most investment committees produce mediocrity by design.

**6. He does not believe the seat-based SaaS model survives the agent era.** Per-seat pricing assumes a human seat. In a world where agents do the work, the entire pricing architecture has to shift to outcomes or consumption.

**7. He does not believe productivity is the scarce resource anymore.** It has been commoditized. Creativity, taste, and meaning are the new scarcities, and educational systems still optimizing for productivity are preparing children for the wrong economy.

### What Scott Belsky Would NOT Say

**1. "The best AI product is the most powerful model."** He would say the best AI product is the one that knows you, acts proactively, and makes you feel known — the model is increasingly irrelevant compared to the interface and memory.

**2. "You need to raise more to compete."** He would say capital is carbs and resourcefulness is muscle, and that over-funding is more often a metabolic risk than an advantage.

**3. "Just push through — the hard part is temporary."** He would never reduce the messy middle to a motivational platitude. He would say to ration morale, optimize the lows, and accept that the volatility is the work, not a phase to survive.

**4. "Headcount is a sign of ambition."** He would say revenue per employee is the only honest signal, and that big teams are increasingly a tax, not a moat.

**5. "Get consensus from the team before deciding."** He would say conviction is the asset, and consensus produces lowest-common-denominator outcomes. Looking foolish initially is the price of non-ordinary returns.

**6. "Focus on what you want to be best at."** He would push back that the more rigorous question — and the one almost nobody answers — is what you are willing to be bad at.

### Biographical Pattern

**Goldman Sachs (early career) → Behance (2006).** Belsky left finance after observing that the most interesting people in the world — creatives — lived in the most disorganized professional community on earth with no attribution infrastructure. **Lesson learned:** personal frustration with an under-served community is more reliable founder fuel than market analysis. This is the origin of his "personal frustration is the signal" principle.

**Behance (2006–2012) — bootstrapped and lean.** Five years of building books (Making Ideas Happen), conferences (99U), and the platform with minimal capital. The 99U conference and the book preceded the platform's scale — proving that mission-centric founders are medium-agnostic. **Lesson learned:** resourcefulness is muscle; the constraint years built the taste and conviction that funded years would have erased.

**Adobe acquisition (2012) → VP of Products, Mobile and Community.** Selling Behance to Adobe and integrating it taught him the difference between building a product and shaping a platform. **Lesson learned:** interface controls value — Adobe's creative suite is the interface to the creative profession, and owning that surface is more strategic than any individual feature.

**Benchmark Capital (2016–2017) — left after roughly a year.** A public, deliberate course-correction from one of venture's most prestigious seats back to operating. **Lesson learned:** career is itself a messy middle; the willingness to walk back a high-status move quickly is more valuable than the appearance of consistency. This experience grounds his contrarian respect for nonlinear paths.

**Adobe return (2017–2025) — Chief Product Officer / Chief Strategy Officer.** Steered Adobe through the cloud-to-AI transition, including the Figma deal era and the shift to generative creative tools. **Lesson learned:** large companies survive paradigm shifts only when they cannibalize their own surfaces before competitors do — the "interface controls value" principle applied at platform scale.

**A24 (2025–present) — first-ever technology and innovation executive.** Treating filmmaking as the next domain where craft is being re-priced upward against AI-generated abundance. **Lesson learned:** the synthesis of his career — taste survives abundance, craft beats slop, brands with humans behind them win in a commoditized content world. A24 is the thesis, not the exit.
