**Alex:** What's up, everyone? Welcome back to another episode of _Founder’s Journal_ and the second episode of _Founder’s Journal_, rebooted. I'm your host, Alex Lieberman, co-founder and executive chairman of Morning Brew. As I mentioned last episode, _Founder’s Journal_ is back and better than ever with a new format that I know you're going to love. Each week I am curating a world-class entrepreneur and interviewing them about their one thing, their one superpower, that one thing that looks effortless to the world, the one thing that stacks the deck in their favor as they build great businesses.

And today's guest is my good friend, Jesse Pujji. Jesse is a successful serial entrepreneur and content creator. He's the co-founder of Ampush, one of the first and largest Facebook paid marketing agencies, which he sold to Red Ventures, and he's also the founder of Gateway X, a startup studio with successful companies like GrowthAssistant, Ox Insights, and Unbloat. This episode we talk about Jesse's one thing, which is using entrepreneurship as a vehicle for personal growth. We talk about how Jesse has become a stronger leader, partner, and person by following the tools and tactics of the Conscious Leadership Group. This includes things like Zone of Genius, five levels of motivation, above the line/below the line, context versus content, and more. So without further ado, here's my conversation with Jesse Pujji.

Okay, so I texted you saying, what is your one thing, your one superpower that you wanna talk about on the podcast? And you said, I think, personal growth. I started writing a TED Talk. Here's the first draft. Can you explain what personal growth as your one thing means? Like what does that mean to you when you say personal growth is your thing?

**Jesse Pujji:** Yeah, I think just being the best version of yourself. I think everyone talks about how they have the things that make them great and their strengths and how they show up. Everyone has a day, everyone listening could think of a day where they were like, man, I was at my best. And I think the reality is we don't always show up as our best every day, both inside and outside. And the ability to do that all the time, and then that obviously benefits yourself, but it benefits everyone around you and uplifts others around you. And so how do you show up as the best version of who you are with those traits? And obviously that part of that means dropping self-criticism, dropping the imposter syndrome, like there's all these other things kind of going on in everyone internally that I wanna keep improving my ability to show up as the best version of myself every day.

**Alex:** Do you have any stories that come to mind when you think about moments in your entrepreneurial journey that led to kind of your greatest inflection points of self-growth or self-discovery?

**Jesse:** Yeah, you know, one interesting story, and I know we're gonna get into this more, but you know, I ran the same business for 10 years, and the way you solve problems in that business is the same over the 10 years. You know, something's going wrong with somebody's business, their revenue, the account, the marketing, you dig in, you understand what levers are driving it, how could they be fixed? And one of my employees, Adoni, who's a good friend, worked for me for six or seven years. He was taking me on a walk, you know, after I had started coaching. So I had started coaching in 2017, this was like 2019 and just catching up, how are you? And he goes, I can give you a piece of feedback, Jesse. He goes, I don't know what you've done, but when I meet with you now and something's going wrong, you don't say anything different than you used to say. But I feel completely different walking out of that meeting. And I tell that story, I've written about it, I've told him, it really hit me and touched me because it really, you know, the point he was making was like, when something's broken, the way you triage it logically is no different. But what energy are you bringing into the room? And I think I used to bring a ton of, I'll call it “me energy,” but it's just like the same thing as fear energy, right? I'm afraid it's not gonna be successful. There's an issue. And if you work for someone and they walk in with that energy, man, it's scary also, right? Like, am I gonna get fired? Is this guy mad at whatever? And you know, actually one tactical thing I did do that was kind of interesting that Dave, you know, our mutual coach taught me, he's like, just put index cards in your back pocket around what you wanna show up like, because I don't need to teach you how to show up like a teacher does. You already know how to do that. And I didn't do it in this instance with Adoni, but for a long time I would pull out like, be a coach or be a teacher. And instead, you know, when, now when I show up, I try, not always, show up with “you energy,” which is like, oh, hey, he's having a problem. How can I coach and teach him? And I'm trying to help him. And that's how I'm, again, what we spend time on, mathematic logic, it's all the same, right? So that's a story that I always tell, and it really for myself was a great guidepost.

And was it with Uber, where like there was one account that basically you were about to lose?
