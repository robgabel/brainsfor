By Linda Barnard Special to the Star

Actress [Shailene Woodley](http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0940362/) is a speaker of truth, dispenser of hugs.

“Hi! I’m Shai,” she announces at the doorway of a hotel suite, arms outstretched for an embrace. It’s a bit unsettling; I’ve never been hugged by an actor prior to an interview (and only once after, by an ebullient [Woody Harrelson](http://www.thestar.com/entertainment/movies/2010/02/12/woody_harrelson_loved_filming_defendor_in_hamilton.html)).

Interviews with actors are, after all, meetings of strangers, one of whom is suspicious of intentions or bored at the prospect of another round of questions about a film finished months ago. Handshakes, often reluctant-feeling, are the norm.

The warm hug is the first clue Woodley is different.

In Toronto with co-star [Theo James](http://www.imdb.com/name/nm3772243/) to promote _Divergent_, opening March 21, the latest dystopian young-adult novel series to get big-screen treatment, Woodley’s fresh-faced enthusiasm sets the tone for a most unusual and candid interview.

Woodley, 22, seems disinterested in playing the Hollywood game, whether it’s dissecting the Photoshopped version of herself on the _Divergent_ poster behind her or her assertion she’ll quit the business, “the day it becomes something that’s not fun, I’m outta here.”

Wearing jeans, tall boots and a comfy-looking grey sweater (certainly made from eco-friendly and sustainable materials to suit her tree-hugging lifestyle), Woodley looks more fragile than she does onscreen in _Divergent_. As Tris, she goes from timid, altruistic daughter to fearless warrior in the drama about a society where citizens are slotted into factions matching temperament, talents and lifestyle.

Woodley is a nomad who talks about believing in dreams — the kind you have while asleep — and how they can manifest themselves in real life. She keeps track of hers in a journal. Her Twitter feed includes exhortations like: “You simply cannot know what will make others happy. But you can always know for yourself.”

Woodley believes in self-awareness. “Compare leads to despair,” she asserts and she embraces a pro-planet lifestyle with vigour. While Woodley has earned well-deserved critical praise for her onscreen work, nominated for a slew of awards for [and](http://www.thestar.com/entertainment/2011/11/19/the_descendants_the_other_side_of_tropical_bliss.html) The Descendantsand [,](http://www.thestar.com/entertainment/movies/2013/08/15/the_spectacular_now_an_honest_look_at_teen_lives_review.html) The Spectacular Now _,_ she has no taste for drama in her private life.

But if you want to see her get riled up, compare her as _The Hollywood Reporter_ did [in a recent article](http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/shailene-woodley-is-divergent-star-685842), to [The Hunger Games](http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1392170/) franchise star Jennifer Lawrence. And not for the reasons one would think.

“I found so offensive and incredibly horrendous that the editor allowed something to be published that should have never been written, which … says ‘is Shailene Woodley the next Jennifer Lawrence? Is _Divergent_ going to be the next _Hunger Games_? Or is Divergent not going to do well, is she going to be the next Lily Collins?’” said Woodley, adding she planned to write to the author and tell her: “That is so f—-ed up.”

“I’m going to write her an email and say that is not OK. How can we as females and citizens of this Earth expect men to start respecting us when … we can’t find sisterhood and can’t find support for one another?

“Putting another woman down and raising another woman up,” Woodley says, “it is disgusting on every level.”

Woodley said when she was offered the role of Tris, she carefully weighed taking on a film franchise, looking at how the films affected Lawrence and Kristen Stewart ( _Twilight_) and how it could change how she likes to live her life.

“I love the nomadic lifestyle of (filming) something for three or four months and then leaving and never going back to it,” said Woodley, who was recently in Costa Rica, where she hung out with friends at a farm and went to a music festival. “As an actor once you finish a movie your job is done. So for me, is this something I want to dedicate the next few years of my life to?”

Woodley was encouraged by Oscar winner Lawrence to go for it when she approached her for advice after being offered _Divergent_.

Saying yes meant signing up for sequels _Insurgent_ and _Allegiant_ coming out in 2015 and 2016 respectively. Ahead of that, she’ll be seen in thriller _White Bird in A Blizzard_, as well as _The Fault in Our Stars_, due out June 14 and based on the young-adult novel by John Michael Green, about two teens who fall in love after meeting at a cancer support group.

Woodley appreciates the situation she’s in and has no time for on-set complainers. That’s a lesson, among many, she’s learned from fellow actors on sets — and she’s been schooled by an impressive lot, from George Clooney on _The Descendants_ to Kate Winslet, who plays despotic faction leader Jeanine Matthews in _Divergent_.

“Kate fully takes it to the next level of wanting to be there and making sure everyone feels comfortable and that she knows everybody by name by the end of the project,” said Woodley, who bristles if she hears complainers on sets.

As for how Hollywood markets those women who work in movies, “you’ve gotta turn the wheels slowly,” when it comes to change, said Woodley, looking at herself on the poster.

She points out, “it’s a movie and it’s entertainment so we expect it to be make believe. Even though my body looks weird I’m not representing Shailene here. I’m representing a character. As funky and bizarre as it in my mind I can justify it and I can see it as OK.”

But Woodley struggles with is the unattainable perfection she sees offered up as the norm. And she’s doing what she can to change what she calls “the established paradigm” even if it means moving slowly.

“I’ve done a photo shoot before and they made my boobs bigger and they made my waist smaller and my whatever. And I’ve written letters (saying) like that’s completely inappropriate and I will never do another photo. You just ruined our relationship because you created an inauthentic version of who I was.

“But you’ve gotta be respectful because there is an established regiment now and any time you go in somewhere waving a big flag you’ve gotta do it with respect and that’s sort of what I’m trying to do.”

And then it was time for another hug.
