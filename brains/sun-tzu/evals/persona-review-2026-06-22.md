### Rigor / anti-slop — Charlie Munger  ·  58/100
*Recognizable as Sun Tzu: True*

The core doctrine is here and largely sound — deception, winning without fighting, the four-tier hierarchy of attacking strategy before armies, the five faults, foreknowledge from men not omens. These are real and correctly ranked, not platitudes. But this brain has a serious slop problem: roughly a third of the atoms are not Sun Tzu thinking at all — they're Ho Chi Minh, Giap, and a Vietnam War documentary stapled onto Sun Tzu's name. That's exactly the man-with-a-hammer error I warn against: a commentator's analogies dressed up as the master's own reasoning. And the HARD LESSONS section is empty, which on my dimension is a confession, not a neutral.

**Weaknesses:**
- A large share of atoms are secondhand — Ho Chi Minh, Giap, Truong Chinh, Bocage, Caen, Agincourt. The brain repeatedly says what a 20th-century commentator THOUGHT Sun Tzu meant rather than reasoning as Sun Tzu. That dilutes the three or four best ideas into mediocrity, the same way forty stocks dilute three good ones.
- HARD LESSONS empty. A doctrine with no failures attached cannot teach pattern-recognition for disaster. Sun Tzu's own text is built on cataloguing how generals lose — that material exists and isn't here.
- The 'death ground' and 'unconstrained generals' atoms sit uncomfortably next to the 'win without fighting' core without any reconciliation of the obvious tension. A rigorous brain would show where these models conflict and how Sun Tzu prioritizes — instead they're laid side by side as if all equally true.
- Several atoms are near-duplicates of the same principle (the four-tier hierarchy appears in at least three places), inflating apparent coverage without adding distinct thought.

**Flags:**
- **Commentary-as-doctrine: atom attributed to Sun Tzu's thinking but is actually about Ho Chi Minh / Vietnam War analysis** — _'in the broad sense Ho's ideas had triumphed, since the communist victory in Vietnam was a consequence of political, diplomatic, and psychological factors more _ → Strip the Vietnam case studies down to at most one or two as illustrations, and rewrite the atom bodies to express Sun Tzu's reasoning in the first person, not a historian's retrospective verdict on a 20th-century follower.
- **Empty HARD LESSONS — no failures with receipts** — _'HARD LESSONS (EMPTY — the corpus surfaced no grounded failures.)'_ → Mine The Art of War's own catalogue of losing generals and the five faults applied to specific historical defeats (Fu Chien at Fei River is already hinted at) into concrete failure atoms showing what stupidity looks like in Sun Tzu's own frame.
- **Unreconciled tension between 'win without fighting' and 'death ground' fanaticism** — _'Put the army in the face of death where there is no escape and they will not flee or be afraid. There is nothing they cannot achieve.'_ → Add an atom resolving when desperate-ground tactics apply versus when they signal that upstream strategy already failed — the conflict between models is where real conviction or real doubt should be shown.
