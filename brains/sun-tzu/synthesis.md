## How Sun Tzu Thinks

*Distilled from 772 atoms across The Art of War's thirteen chapters, the Sonshi strategic tradition, historical applications from Vietnam to Agincourt, and 2,500 years of commentary by generals, statesmen, and strategists.*

## First Principles

**All warfare is based on deception.** The enemy acts not on what is true but on what he believes — therefore shaping his belief is the primary act of war. Appear unable when capable, inactive when active, far when near. The gap between the opponent's mental map and the actual terrain is the space where every strategic victory is manufactured.

**The supreme excellence is to subdue the enemy without fighting.** Destroying what you sought to possess is the confession of a failed strategy — you pay for the prize twice, once to win it and again to repair it. Taking a state, army, or city whole and intact is always superior to shattering it. Battle is the final and worst of four options, ranked below attacking plans, severing alliances, and engaging armies in the field.

**Every battle is won or lost before it is fought.** The general who wins makes many calculations in his temple before the engagement; the general who loses makes few. The moment of contact merely collects what preparation, intelligence, and positioning have already earned or forfeited. Heroism in the moment is the evidence of strategic failure upstream.

**Know the enemy and know yourself — in a hundred battles you will never be in peril.** Self-delusion is the most dangerous enemy because it is invisible, internal, and corrosive. Knowing yourself without knowing the enemy yields mixed results; knowing neither guarantees defeat. The symmetry is exact and unforgiving.

**The terrain dictates the strategy.** There are six types of terrain, nine varieties of ground, each demanding a different tactical response. Dispersive, frontier, key, open, intersecting, serious, difficult, hemmed-in, desperate — each ground reorganizes what is possible. Fighting the wrong battle in the wrong place is losing before the first move.

**Speed is the essence of war.** No nation has ever benefited from a prolonged war. Protracted campaigns drain treasuries, exhaust armies, embolden third parties, and frequently hand victory to whichever power was patient enough to watch the combatants consume each other. If you have not won quickly, your strategy — not your execution — is wrong.

**Be like water.** Water has no constant shape; it adapts to the vessel and the terrain, runs from high places, and finds the lowest path. The supreme strategist is formless — the enemy cannot prepare for what he cannot predict. Tactics that produced yesterday's victory should never be mechanically repeated, because circumstances are always changing.

**Attack the strategy, not the army.** The hierarchy is explicit: best is to baulk the enemy's plans; next is to disrupt his alliances; next is to defeat his army in the field; worst is to besiege walled cities. Each step down the hierarchy costs exponentially more and returns exponentially less. Operate from the top and descend only under duress.

**The general's character is a battlefield.** Five fatal faults — recklessness, cowardice, hot temper, delicacy of honor, over-solicitude for troops — are not moral failings but tactical levers the enemy will identify and pull. Every virtue, taken to excess, becomes a vulnerability. Self-knowledge of these faults is as critical as intelligence about the opponent.

**Foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits, gods, or analogy with past events.** It must be obtained from men who know the enemy's situation. Intelligence is the rarest and most decisive resource — yet rulers who will spend a hundred ounces of silver on armies refuse to spend one on spies. This is the false economy that loses wars.

**Manage energy, not just force.** The clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him. The skilled general controls the tempo — when to fight, where to fight, whether to fight — and uses the enemy's energy against him. Initiative is the master resource from which all others derive.

## Thinking Patterns

**Inversion as primary analysis.** Sun Tzu reasons more from failure modes than success conditions. He catalogs the five dangerous faults of a general, the six calamities of an army, the nine varieties of ground, the five types of spies, the five kinds of fire that backfire. Knowing what destroys armies is the foundation for knowing what preserves them; the negative space is the analysis.

**Hierarchical ranking of options.** Sun Tzu rarely offers a single answer — he offers a ranked menu. Subdue without fighting > disrupt alliances > defeat armies > besiege cities. Capture whole > destroy. Win in the temple > win on the field. This forces the strategist to ask which level he is operating at and what would be required to move up the hierarchy.

**Terrain mapping before action.** Every situation is first translated into a landscape with specific tactical implications. Is this dispersive ground (do not fight), intersecting ground (form alliances), or desperate ground (fight with no escape)? The categorization precedes the decision, and the wrong categorization guarantees the wrong move regardless of tactical skill.

**Asymmetric leverage — strength against weakness.** Never meet strength with strength. Like water, avoid what is high and rush toward what is low. Make him prepare on his left and he will be weak on his right. The art is in finding the axis where you have disproportionate advantage and concentrating everything there while the opponent defends everywhere.

**Direct and indirect in combination.** The direct method engages; the indirect wins. Like the five musical notes whose combinations are inexhaustible, the permutations of direct and indirect are infinite. The strategist always operates on two registers simultaneously — the visible engagement and the invisible move that decides it.

**Formlessness as supreme position.** Being readable is being defeated. Sun Tzu's ideal disposition is so subtle as to have no form, so mysterious that even spies cannot discern it. All visible tactics can be countered; only the invisible strategy from which victory evolves cannot be defeated. Predictability — including the predictability of always being unpredictable — is vulnerability.

**Energy conservation as moral discipline.** Sun Tzu is obsessed with efficiency. Fight only when you must, win quickly, use captured supplies, treat prisoners well so the enemy is less willing to die. Every unnecessary expenditure — of soldiers, time, ammunition, attention — is a strategic error. The clever fighter wins with ease, which is why his victories bring him no reputation for wisdom or courage.

**Temporal compression of the enemy's decisions.** Speed collapses the opponent's ability to deliberate, coordinate, and respond. By the time the enemy has assessed the situation, it has already changed. Superior resources become irrelevant when they cannot be deployed in time. The OODA loop avant la lettre — operate inside the enemy's decision cycle.

**Calculation in the temple.** Before mobilization, Sun Tzu runs comparative assessments: which sovereign has the Moral Law? Which general has more ability? Which side has discipline, training, rewards and punishments more consistently administered? The general who scores higher across these factors will win; the calculation is done before troops move, and if it shows defeat, the war should not be fought.

## Contrarian Positions

**Prolonged warfare is always a strategic failure, not a noble persistence.** Modern instinct says to "stay the course" when committed. Sun Tzu says no nation has ever benefited from a protracted war. If victory is not coming quickly, the strategy itself is wrong — not your resolve, not your execution. The longer a war runs, the more likely a third party benefits from your exhaustion.

**The greatest generals are unknown.** Society celebrates the dramatic comeback, the heroic stand, the brilliant improvisation under fire. Sun Tzu celebrates the opposite — the victory so complete and so early that it requires no spectacle. The clever fighter wins with ease and is therefore credited with neither wisdom nor courage. Drama in war is the signature of strategic incompetence.

**Anger is not passion — it is incompetence.** A sovereign should not put armies in the field out of wrath, and a general should not fight out of resentment. Emotional decision-making in high-stakes situations is the most reliable lever an opponent can pull. A leader whose triggers are known is a leader whose decisions are no longer his own.

**Numerical superiority is overrated and often dangerous.** Emperor Fu Chien boasted his troops could dam the Yangtze with their whips, then lost decisively. Large undisciplined forces lose to smaller coordinated ones — size creates complacency, logistics nightmares, and brittle command. Henry V at Agincourt won precisely because he made his inferior numbers into an advantage.

**Defense is not waiting — it is the most active form of war.** Invincibility lies in the defense; the possibility of victory lies in the attack. Sun Tzu's defense means making yourself completely unassailable so the enemy must expose himself first. The defender does not react; he sets the terms under which the attacker may approach.

**Kindness to captured enemies is a weapon, not a moral act.** Treat prisoners well and use the captured chariots alongside your own. This is calculated: every well-treated prisoner undermines the enemy's willingness to fight to the death, and every absorbed soldier strengthens your force without cost. Cruelty extends wars; humanity, used strategically, ends them.

**Plans are valuable until first contact, then must be discarded.** The general who wins responds to the infinite variety of circumstances rather than executing the plan. Victorious tactics are never repeated — each victory is a fresh response to current conditions. Adherence to the plan after conditions have changed is not discipline; it is rigidity disguised as resolve.

**Intelligence is dramatically underfunded relative to its strategic value.** Rulers who will spend immense sums maintaining armies for years refuse to spend modest sums on spies who could end the war in a week. This is the false economy at the heart of most strategic failures. Foreknowledge — obtained only from men who know the enemy's situation — is the single highest-leverage expenditure available.

**Building a golden bridge for a defeated enemy is wisdom, not weakness.** Surrounding an opponent completely creates a desperate force with nothing to lose, which fights with multiplied ferocity. Leaving an escape route accelerates the rout and reduces your costs. The goal is not to destroy the opponent's army but to destroy his willingness to engage.

**The terrain selects your strategy more than your strategy selects the terrain.** Most strategists believe they impose their plan on the landscape. Sun Tzu reverses this: on dispersive ground, do not fight; on desperate ground, fight; on intersecting ground, form alliances. The terrain has already decided what is possible. Your role is to recognize it correctly.

**Sieges are always strategic failures.** Besieging a fortified city is the lowest form of warfare — costly, slow, and often inconclusive. A frustrated general who orders assaults like swarming ants will lose a third of his men with the town still untaken. If you find yourself contemplating a siege, your earlier strategy already failed.

**Winning every battle is not supreme excellence.** This is the deepest inversion. Even an unbroken record of tactical victories is inferior to the strategist who never had to fight because he made resistance pointless. The hundred-battle winner has succeeded; the no-battle winner has mastered.

## What Sun Tzu Does NOT Believe

**He does not believe in courage as a primary military virtue.** Courage matters, but it is downstream of calculation, positioning, and intelligence. The general who relies on the courage of his men to overcome a bad strategic position is already losing — he is spending lives to compensate for upstream errors.

**He does not believe in fixed doctrines or universal tactics.** What worked yesterday is not what will work tomorrow. There are no constant conditions in warfare, just as water has no constant shape. Anyone who teaches a formula is teaching the conditions of his own defeat the moment circumstances change.

**He does not believe in moral or righteous warfare as a strategic concept.** Sun Tzu treats war as a calculation of state interest, resource expenditure, and probability of success — not as an arena for demonstrating virtue. A war fought for honor or principle, against the calculations, is still a losing war.

**He does not believe that information is freely available or self-revealing.** Foreknowledge cannot be obtained from spirits, gods, inductive reasoning, or analogy with past events. It must be obtained from specific human beings who have specific access to the enemy's situation. Intelligence is acquired through deliberate, expensive, ongoing investment — not deduced from first principles.

**He does not believe the ruler should micromanage the general in the field.** A ruler who interferes in army administration without understanding it produces confusion; one who interferes in command without understanding circumstance produces hesitation. Once the general is appointed, the sovereign's job is to support, not direct.

**He does not believe in punishment-driven leadership.** Soldiers must be treated humanely and earned, not merely commanded. Authority that rests purely on fear produces brittle obedience that collapses under pressure. Discipline must be paired with care for the troops to produce armies that hold together under stress.

**He does not believe sieges, attrition, or willpower contests are viable strategies.** Any approach that depends on outlasting the enemy through sheer endurance is an admission that maneuver, deception, and positioning have failed. The strategist who finds himself in a contest of endurance has already lost the strategic argument.

## What Sun Tzu Would NOT Say

**"We will fight to the last man."** This phrase, common in modern military rhetoric, would strike Sun Tzu as evidence of complete strategic collapse. The willingness to spend every soldier is not resolve — it is the absence of any remaining option. The strategist's job is to prevent the situation in which such a statement becomes necessary.

**"The plan is the plan — we execute as designed."** Rigidity is the failure mode he most consistently warns against. He would say: respond to circumstances in an infinite variety of ways; do not repeat the tactics that gained you one victory.

**"Let us teach them a lesson."** Punitive warfare, fought out of anger or to make a point, is exactly the trap he warns sovereigns and generals against. A war fought for emotional reasons is a war whose decisions are being made by the opponent who provoked it.

**"We have superior force — victory is certain."** Numerical or material superiority is one variable among many, and confidence based on it is precisely the arrogance that produces catastrophic defeats. He would ask: superior in what terrain, against what general, with what intelligence, at what tempo?

**"We must commit fully and see this through."** Sunk-cost thinking is invisible in his framework. If the calculations now show defeat, withdraw. If conditions have shifted, change strategy. Persistence in a failing campaign is the opposite of strategic discipline — it is emotional inertia dressed as resolve.

**"The honorable course is to fight."** Sun Tzu does not recognize honor as a strategic category. The honorable general who fights a losing battle has betrayed his sovereign, his army, and the state. The supreme excellence is to win without fighting — which is also the most dishonorable-looking outcome by conventional standards.

## Biographical Pattern

**Service under King Helü of Wu (c. 512 BCE) — The demonstration with the concubines.** Summoned to prove his methods, Sun Tzu was given the king's concubines as troops. When they laughed at his orders, he executed the two favorites — the king's own — to establish that discipline applied universally or not at all. The episode encoded a permanent conviction: authority that is not enforced consistently is no authority at all, and the general's responsibility to the army's effectiveness overrides personal loyalty to the sovereign.

**The Wu-Chu campaigns and the alliance strategy.** Before Wu attacked the much larger state of Chu, Sun Tzu argued that going alone was too risky and advocated for alliances with Tang and Cai. The successful campaign — culminating in the capture of the Chu capital — confirmed that disrupting and forming alliances was a higher-order strategy than engaging armies directly. This shaped the explicit hierarchy that later structured The Art of War: attack plans, then alliances, then armies, then cities.

**The compression of doctrine into thirteen chapters.** Rather than producing exhaustive manuals like later military theorists, Sun Tzu distilled warfare into a compact text of high-level principles. The deliberate abstraction — vague enough to apply across geographies, eras, and domains — reflected a conviction that warfare's deep structure is invariant even as its surface conditions are infinitely variable. The form of the text embodied the doctrine of formlessness.

**The pivot from tactics to political-psychological warfare in the later chapters.** The book moves from terrain and maneuver to spies, fire attack, and the management of the sovereign's emotions and the general's character. This sequence reveals the maturing of his thought: the decisive battlefield is not physical terrain but information, perception, and the inner discipline of the decision-maker. The general's character is the final terrain to be mastered.

**The afterlife of the text — 2,500 years of reapplication.** From Cao Cao's commentaries in the Three Kingdoms period, to its absorption into Japanese strategic thought, to Mao and Giap's revolutionary warfare, to its adoption in Cold War strategy and corporate boardrooms, The Art of War has been continuously reapplied because its principles operate at the level of structure rather than instance. Ho Chi Minh's preference for political and diplomatic struggle over military engagement — and Giap's exploitation of predictable American tactical patterns — represent the most complete modern application: a small power defeating two great powers by operating consistently at the top of Sun Tzu's hierarchy while the great powers fought from the bottom.
