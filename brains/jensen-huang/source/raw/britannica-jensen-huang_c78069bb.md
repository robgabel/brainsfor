**Jensen Huang** (born February 17, 1963, [Tainan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tai-nan-Taiwan), Taiwan) is a Taiwanese-born American entrepreneur who cofounded the American [semiconductor](https://www.britannica.com/science/semiconductor) company [NVIDIA Corporation](https://www.britannica.com/money/NVIDIA-Corporation). Under Huang’s leadership, NVIDIA has become one of the leading providers of [graphics processing units](https://www.britannica.com/technology/graphics-processing-unit) (GPUs) and has taken center stage in the current artificial intelligence (AI) boom.

Huang was born the second son of [Taiwan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Taiwan) citizens Huang Hsing-tai and Lo Tsai-hsiu. Huang’s father was a chemical engineer, and his mother taught [grade school](https://www.britannica.com/topic/graded-school). When he was five, the family moved to [Thailand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand). However, due to the then-ongoing [Vietnam War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Vietnam-War), Huang’s parents decided that they wouldn’t settle in Thailand permanently. Huang’s father had previously been accepted into a worker-training program at the American [air conditioner](https://www.britannica.com/technology/air-conditioning) manufacturer Carrier and had formed a positive impression of the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). Huang’s mother helped prepare Huang and his brother for the eventual move by teaching them 10 random English words a day.

When Huang was nine years old, he and his brother were sent to live with their uncle in [Tacoma](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tacoma), [Washington](https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-state), while their parents stayed in Thailand. Shortly thereafter, Huang’s uncle sent them to the all-boys Oneida Baptist Institute in rural Oneida, [Kentucky](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kentucky), mistakenly believing it to be a prestigious [boarding school](https://www.britannica.com/topic/boarding-school). In fact, the institute was a religious [reform school](https://www.britannica.com/topic/reformatory) for “difficult” children. Huang was forced to clean toilets every day while his brother worked on a tobacco farm. The boys were relentlessly bullied by fellow students, were called ethnic slurs, and were even threatened with knives.

About two years later, Huang’s parents arrived in the United States, discovered the situation, and pulled their children out of Oneida. The reunited family settled in the suburbs of Portland, [Oregon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oregon-state), where Huang attended Aloha High School. While there, Huang played competitive [table tennis](https://www.britannica.com/sports/table-tennis) and earned a national ranking. He graduated in 1981 and subsequently attended [Oregon State University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oregon-State-University) (OSU). At OSU, Huang met Lori Mills, a fellow engineering student, and the pair later married.

Huang graduated with a [bachelor’s degree](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bachelors-degree) in [electrical engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electrical-and-electronics-engineering) from OSU in 1984. He moved to [Silicon Valley](https://www.britannica.com/place/Silicon-Valley-region-California), where he took a job with [Advanced Micro Devices](https://www.britannica.com/money/Advanced-Micro-Devices-Inc). He remained there for about a year before accepting a job at the LSI Logic Corporation, where he rose through the ranks and eventually became director of a company division. While working, he returned to school and obtained a [master’s degree](https://www.britannica.com/topic/master-degree) in electrical engineering from [Stanford University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Stanford-University).

In April 1993 Huang founded [NVIDIA](https://www.britannica.com/money/NVIDIA-Corporation) with fellow microchip designers Chris Malachowsky and Curtis Priem, who selected Huang to serve as [CEO](https://www.britannica.com/money/chief-executive-officer). The trio aimed to create GPUs for the [video game](https://www.britannica.com/topic/electronic-game) industry. The trio began with $40,000 in capital but soon procured an initial investment of $20 million from [venture capital](https://www.britannica.com/money/venture-capital) firms. With this backing, NVIDIA survived a difficult first few years and eventually gained a solid position in the semiconductor market. The company went public in 1999. When its stock price hit $100 per share, Huang got a [tattoo](https://www.britannica.com/art/tattoo) of the company’s logo on his left shoulder.

Huang got a tattoo of the NVIDIA company logo when the company’s stock price hit $100 per share, but he has admitted that he would most likely not get another tattoo.

NVIDIA grew beyond the gaming industry, reaching into fields such as [artificial intelligence](https://www.britannica.com/technology/artificial-intelligence), mobile computing, [autonomous vehicle](https://www.britannica.com/technology/autonomous-vehicle) technology, and [social networking](https://www.britannica.com/technology/social-network). In fact, with its powerful GPUs, NVIDIA helped to power the AI revolution. Such chips, though they were initially designed for game development, were also discovered to be viable candidates for running [machine learning](https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine-learning) algorithms. Huang’s wealth and profile grew in tandem. The worth of his 3.5 percent of NVIDIA stock made him a billionaire, and he became one of the highest-paid CEOs in the United States.

In 2007 Huang and his wife founded the [philanthropic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philanthropy) Jen-Hsun and Lori Huang Foundation, which made donations to the Huangs’ alma maters, to [public health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/public-health) initiatives, and to various San Francisco Bay Area organizations. In 2017 _Fortune_ magazine named Huang its Businessperson of the Year, and in 2019 _Harvard Business Review_ ranked him as the world’s best-performing CEO. In 2021 _[Time](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Time-American-magazine)_ magazine included him on its annual list of the world’s hundred most influential people.

At the 2018 GPU Technology Conference held by NVIDIA in San Jose, Huang remarked that NVIDIA’s GPUs were 25 times faster than they had been five years prior and thus were advancing more rapidly than [Moore’s law](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Moores-law) had suggested that they would. The press immediately dubbed this observation—that GPU performance was more than doubling every two years—“Huang’s law.”
