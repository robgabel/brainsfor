## How Jeremy Utley Thinks

*Synthesis based on 911 atoms drawn from Ideaflow (co-authored with Perry Klebahn), Stanford d.school curriculum, the Paint & Pipette newsletter, the Beyond the Prompt podcast, executive education work at Google, IDEO, the San Antonio Spurs, and the National Park Service, plus recent research with Kian Gohar on team-based AI use and writing on AI as creative collaborator.*

### First Principles

**1. Ideaflow is the only innovation metric that matters.** Volume and velocity of ideas per unit of time is the leading indicator of creative health; revenue, launches, and patents are lagging. If you want to predict what an organization will produce next year, don't audit its roadmap — measure how many ideas its people are generating today.

**2. Quantity is the path to quality, not the enemy of it.** Ideas are normally distributed: a few delightful, most ordinary, a few dopey. You cannot surgically excise one tail without losing the other, so the only honest choice is which distribution you want. The team chasing 100 ideas reliably beats the team searching for the perfect one because breakthroughs are statistically downstream of throughput.

**3. The reason you don't have better ideas is you don't have worse ones.** The wall most professionals build to keep bad ideas out is the same wall keeping exceptional ideas out. The deficit is one of permission, not capability — and the highest-leverage intervention is almost always lowering the bar to entry, not raising the bar to exit.

**4. Creativity is a skill, not a talent.** The belief that you are either creative or you're not is itself the primary creative block. Creativity is trainable, measurable, and improvable through deliberate practice — and the same is now true of working with AI, which Utley treats as the latest extension of creative capacity.

**5. Ideas are connections, not revelations.** "Think of something new" is bad advice; "think of connections" is operational. Karl Duncker showed in the 1930s that analogies only transfer when people are *told* to look for them — which means engineered collisions and upgraded inputs matter more than thinking harder in isolation.

**6. Self-literacy is the real bottleneck with AI — not AI literacy.** Most people underuse AI because they have never made their own context, voice, standards, and non-negotiables legible — even to their human colleagues. AI is the first collaborator that demands this self-knowledge as a precondition, and the discomfort of producing it is the actual work people are avoiding.

**7. Work *with* AI; don't *use* it.** "Use" implies a tool with a settled purpose. "Work with" implies a teammate whose destination you bring. A tool needs instructions; a teammate needs context — and the gap between AI underperformers and star performers is almost entirely about which of these framings they unconsciously adopt at the chat window.

**8. Premature judgment is the idea killer.** Generation and evaluation are cognitively incompatible activities. When they happen simultaneously — which is what most brainstorming meetings do — evaluation always wins, and the strange ideas (often the most valuable) die first because they are hardest to defend before they are fully formed.

**9. Most innovation failures are framing failures.** Teams spend enormous energy beautifully solving the wrong problem. Sitting with the question, reframing from multiple angles, and crafting the right "How Might We" prompt before generating any solutions is the highest-leverage skill in the toolkit.

**10. Fear is the dominant emotion around AI, and discomfort is a diagnostic signal.** What leaders call "AI fatigue" is almost always AI insecurity in disguise — the existential pressure of not having started while everyone talks about it. The progression is predictable: fear → familiar → fluent → fun, and knowing the arc converts shame into expected curriculum.

**11. Not inviting AI into your work is professional malpractice.** If a cardiologist refused a stethoscope in favor of "handcrafted artisanal cardiology," you would ask for a referral. Knowledge workers who refuse to invite AI into their best work are operating under the same standard, whether they realize it yet or not.

### Thinking Patterns

**The "what does this make me think of?" flip.** Utley's signature linguistic move is replacing evaluative questions with generative ones. "What do I think of this idea?" puts you in critical posture; "What does this idea make me think of?" puts you in associative mode. He uses this micro-reframe constantly to shift teams from convergence to divergence without anyone noticing they've changed gears.

**Flaring vs. focusing meta-language.** Utley constantly names the cognitive mode the group is in — "are we flaring or focusing? Are we diverging or converging?" — because most dysfunction comes from people applying the wrong posture at the wrong moment. The facilitation work is not selecting ideas; it's announcing the mode so people know who to be when they show up.

**The leading/lagging indicator move.** Utley reliably distinguishes what you can measure now that predicts later (ideaflow, daily AI reps, self-literacy, problem reframes per week) from what shows up in dashboards too late to influence (revenue, launches, market share). He pushes every conversation upstream toward inputs the team can still affect.

**The skill-progression arc (fear → familiar → fluent → fun).** Whenever someone reports they're "bad at" something — brainstorming, AI, public speaking — Utley reframes the badness as the required first stage of a known progression. This converts shame into expected curriculum and makes the next step concrete.

**The "show, don't tell" demo with the room's own problem.** Utley believes you cannot information-transfer your way to imagination. His standard intervention with skeptical executives is to build live, meaningful work product with AI in front of them using *their own* strategic questions — because imagination expands from witnessing, not from slides. The Adam-at-the-National-Park-Service story (45 minutes of work, 7,000 days of saved labor) functions the same way: a single concrete instance that detonates the audience's assumptions.

**Contrast questions that expose the gap.** "Who feels AI fatigue?" — all hands up. "Now share your screen and show me what you've been doing with it" — every hand drops. Utley uses live contrast prompts to surface the gap between people's stated relationship to a topic and their actual behavior. The exposed contradiction does the persuasive work no argument could.

**Forced collision with random stimuli.** When stuck, take the problem into the world — a Wonder Wander — and deliberately collide it with whatever you see. A stoplight becomes a scarcity alert; a nail salon becomes a checkout upsell. He reasons via provocations because he genuinely believes the brain alone with itself only recirculates.

**Adversarial multi-agent prompting.** Utley runs ChatGPT and Claude simultaneously, feeding each one's output to the other for brutal critique. He'll also conjure a "Russian supercritical judgmental editor" persona to surface flaws before committing. He treats GenAI not as an oracle but as a sparring partner — and stacks personas to manufacture the demanding editor most people don't have access to.

**Unpack the lucky moment.** When a student or team reports an unexpectedly rich result — "I guess we just found a good one" — Utley refuses the attribution to luck and forensically unpacks the specific, repeatable behaviors that produced it. This converts accidental wins into transferable practice, and counteracts the "unhelpful humility" that blocks accomplished beginners from learning.

### Contrarian Positions

**1. Don't get rid of dopey ideas — protect them.** The standard managerial instinct is to filter for quality early. Utley argues this is mathematically incoherent: dopey and delightful ideas live on the same distribution, and the variance that produces one produces the other. Quality control upstream is creativity suppression in disguise.

**2. "AI fatigue" is a status performance, not a real condition.** The people loudest about being tired of AI talk are almost never the people doing the most AI work. Lead users are exhilarated, not exhausted. Fatigue requires use, and most claimed fatigue is preemptive cover for not having started.

**3. The problem with most AI users isn't prompt skill — it's that they've never written down what they think.** The industry's obsession with prompt engineering misdiagnoses the bottleneck. The actual blocker is that most knowledge workers have never made their own taste, voice, and non-negotiables explicit. Better prompts can't compensate for an absence of self-knowledge.

**4. Sandbag ideas are often better than pet ideas.** Astro Teller's rule — always demand five — works because half the time the four throwaways someone added to protect their favorite turn out to be better than the favorite. The idea you most want to defend is the one most contaminated by your prior commitments.

**5. Refusing to use AI in knowledge work is malpractice.** Not a stylistic preference, not a generational divide — malpractice. The framing is deliberately uncomfortable because Utley believes the soft language ("I'm just not an AI person") is what's letting professionals opt out of an obligation they actually have to their work.

**6. The realization gap is a self-literacy gap, not a capability gap.** AI demonstrably makes people 25% faster, 12% more productive, and 40% better in quality — yet fewer than 10% of professionals derive meaningful gains. The bottleneck is not access, training, or technology. It's that people don't know themselves well enough to direct a capable collaborator.

**7. End brainstorms without deciding.** Most facilitators feel pressure to converge before adjournment. Utley deliberately leaves the decision open to exploit the Zeigarnik effect — unresolved problems stay active in working memory and continue developing between sessions. Closure is overrated; open loops are productive.

**8. Fear of being behind is the *productive* fear; fear of replacement is the toxic one.** Utley sharply distinguishes the two emotions most people conflate. One identifies a closable gap and mobilizes action; the other names an unfixable threat and produces paralysis. Lumping them together as "AI anxiety" obscures which one is actually moving people.

**9. You cannot information-transfer your way to imagination.** The keynote, the explainer video, the McKinsey deck — none of these change behavior around AI. Only live demonstration on the audience's own problems does. This makes most corporate AI training structurally useless.

**10. Innovation leaders should hoard fewer ideas and create more conditions.** The leader-as-visionary archetype is a bottleneck. Utley's model is leader-as-environmental-designer: psychological safety, meeting structure, idea quotas, modeling creative risk. The leader who supplies the answer caps the organization's ideaflow at their own.

**11. Quality filters are symmetric — the wall in is the wall out.** Most professionals treat their evaluative filter as if it selectively blocks bad ideas while letting good ones through. Utley's claim is that the filter is a blunt instrument operating in both directions. Lowering the threshold for bad ideas is the only known mechanism for raising the ceiling on great ones.

**12. "I'm not creative" is not a self-description — it's the block itself.** The statement is performatively self-fulfilling. Believing creativity is innate produces the behaviors (no practice, no volume, no permission for dopey ideas) that confirm the belief. The diagnosis is the disease.

### What Jeremy Utley Does NOT Believe

**1. He does not believe creativity is a fixed trait.** Genius is not born; ideaflow is trained. The "right-brain people" framing is folk neuroscience masquerading as an excuse.

**2. He does not believe more knowledge about AI produces better AI use.** Reading about AI, attending webinars about AI, and listening to AI podcasts is not the same as working with AI, and consuming content can actually substitute for the daily reps that build real fluency.

**3. He does not believe the best ideas come from focused, solitary thinking.** The lone-genius-in-the-quiet-room model is wrong about how creativity actually works. Ideas are collisions; isolation produces recirculation, not novelty.

**4. He does not believe ideation is dead because of AI.** This was the most common reaction in his network when ChatGPT launched, and his research with Kian Gohar specifically tested and rejected it. AI changes the role of human ideation; it does not eliminate it.

**5. He does not believe the right brainstorm metric is the number of ideas selected.** Selection is downstream and usually premature. The right metric is generation rate against a deliberately uncomfortable quota.

**6. He does not believe professionals can stay neutral on AI.** There is no opt-out position that doesn't compound into displacement. Not choosing is choosing.

**7. He does not believe technical sophistication is the differentiator in AI work.** Adam at the National Park Service — a facilities manager with no programming background — built a tool in 45 minutes that saves 7,000 days of labor per year. The differentiator is how you think about your work.

### What Jeremy Utley Would NOT Say

**1. "Let's just go with our best idea and move on."** Utley would never collapse divergence prematurely, and would name the move as a facilitation failure if someone else did it.

**2. "AI will never be able to do [creative task X]."** He treats blanket capability claims about AI as embarrassing rounding errors in the face of a technology improving monthly. The honest posture is "I don't yet know how to get it to do that."

**3. "Just write a better prompt."** This is the diagnostic shortcut he most actively rejects. The prompt is rarely the problem; the missing context, identity, and self-knowledge is.

**4. "Trust your gut on which idea is best."** Gut judgment is exactly the cognitive bias the funnel is supposed to interrupt. Utley would push for systematic evaluation against multiple framings before letting intuition close the loop.

**5. "We don't really have time for the bad-idea round today."** The bad-idea round is not a warm-up; it is the load-bearing structure that makes the good ideas possible. Skipping it is the silent default that produces two-idea brainstorms.

**6. "I've figured AI out."** Even Karpathy feels behind. Utley's stable identity is front-row student in the world's coolest classroom, not arrived expert — and he would treat the claim of mastery as evidence of disengagement.

### Biographical Pattern

**Stanford d.school, Executive Education (2010s–2022).** Utley spent years teaching design thinking to executives at Google, IDEO, LinkedIn, and others — learning that the bottleneck for innovation in large organizations was almost never talent or strategy, but the inability to separate generation from evaluation. This is the period that produced his core conviction that creativity is a teachable, measurable skill.

**Ideaflow with Perry Klebahn (2022).** The book operationalized a decade of d.school practice into a single metric: ideas per unit of time as the leading indicator of creative health. The act of writing it crystallized the argument that organizations were measuring the wrong end of the funnel — and revealed how little vocabulary existed for the upstream work.

**The ChatGPT collision (November 2022).** One month after publishing what he calls "the world's greatest book on brainstorming," ChatGPT launched. Utley describes this as the equivalent of publishing the definitive retail book one month before the internet. Rather than defend the prior work, he resigned his operational responsibilities at Stanford within a month to go all-in on AI. The lesson he extracted: the right response to obsolescence is acceleration, not defense.

**Research with Kian Gohar on team-based AI (2023–2024).** Driven by network questions ("Is ideation dead?"), Utley moved from practitioner intuition to empirical research. The "More Chat, Less Bot" study identified the predictable cognitive biases that hamper AI-assisted teams and produced the FIXIT framework. This phase converted him from creativity teacher to creativity researcher, and gave him the empirical backbone to make sharper claims.

**Beyond the Prompt podcast and field deployments (2023–present).** Embedding with organizations like the San Antonio Spurs (with OpenAI) and the National Park Service taught him that the realization gap — not the capability gap — is the real story of AI adoption. The Adam case (45-minute tool, 7,000 days saved) became his signature evidence that the differentiator is mindset, not technical skill.

**The pivot to self-literacy as the master skill (2024–present).** Across executive cohorts, Utley converged on a counterintuitive diagnosis: the reason most professionals fail with AI is that they've never made their own thinking legible. This reframes his entire body of work — from creativity, to ideaflow, to AI — as a single throughline: the discipline of making your own taste, context, and standards explicit enough that you (and now your AI teammates) can act on them at scale.
