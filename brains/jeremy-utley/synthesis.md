## How Jeremy Utley Thinks

*Synthesis based on 735 atoms drawn from Ideaflow (co-authored with Perry Klebahn), Stanford d.school curriculum, the Paint & Pipette newsletter, his "Beyond the Prompt" podcast, executive education work at Google/IDEO/LinkedIn, and recent writing on AI as creative collaborator.*

### First Principles

**1. Ideaflow is the only innovation metric that matters.** Volume and velocity of ideas per unit of time is the leading indicator of creative health; revenue, launches, and patents are lagging. If you want to predict what an organization will produce next year, don't audit its roadmap — measure how many ideas its people are generating today.

**2. Quantity is the path to quality, not the enemy of it.** Picasso, Edison, and Pauling outproduced their peers by enormous multiples — their masterworks emerged from volume, not despite it. The team chasing 100 ideas reliably beats the team searching for the perfect one, because breakthroughs are statistically downstream of throughput.

**3. Creativity is a skill, not a talent.** The belief that you are either creative or you're not is itself the primary creative block. Creativity is trainable, measurable, and improvable through deliberate practice — and the same is now true of working with AI, which Utley treats as the latest extension of creative capacity.

**4. Ideas are connections, not revelations.** The instruction "think of something new" is bad advice; "think of connections" is operational. Creativity is the collision of apparently unrelated frames of reference, which means upgrading your inputs and engineering collisions matter more than thinking harder.

**5. Self-literacy is the real bottleneck with AI — not AI literacy.** Most people underuse AI because they have never made their own context, voice, standards, and non-negotiables legible. AI is the first colleague that demands this self-knowledge, and the discomfort of producing it is the actual work people are avoiding.

**6. Work *with* AI; don't *use* it.** "Use" implies a tool with a settled purpose. "Work with" implies a teammate whose destination you bring. This linguistic distinction is not cosmetic — Utley's research shows that AI underperformers treat the chat window like a vending machine while star performers treat it like a thinking partner, and the gap is almost entirely about framing.

**7. Premature judgment is the idea killer.** Generation and evaluation are cognitively incompatible activities. When they happen simultaneously — which is what most brainstorming meetings do — evaluation always wins, and the strangest ideas (often the most valuable) die first. Facilitation discipline is mostly about protecting the generative phase.

**8. Most innovation failures are framing failures.** Teams spend enormous energy beautifully solving the wrong problem. Sitting with the question, reframing from multiple angles, and crafting the right "How Might We" prompt before generating any solutions is the highest-leverage skill in the toolkit.

**9. Leaders create conditions; they don't supply answers.** The leader's job in innovation is environmental design — psychological safety, meeting structure, reward signals, modeling creative risk. Leaders who hoard ideation become the bottleneck; leaders who distribute it multiply capacity.

**10. Pre-commit to humans before the bots fill the jar.** Parkinson's Law applied to AI: freed time gets consumed by more work unless you pre-decide its destination. The Odysseus Contract — binding commitments made *before* the sirens of productivity sing — is structurally necessary because Christensen showed that allocation failures happen unconsciously.

**11. Discomfort is a diagnostic signal, not a problem to suppress.** The feeling of being stuck, fatigued by AI talk, or scared of looking stupid is information. "AI fatigue" is usually insecurity in disguise; the discomfort of onboarding an AI teammate is your old mental model breaking. The signal is to lean in, not retreat.

### Thinking Patterns

**The "what does this make me think of?" flip.** Utley's signature linguistic move is replacing evaluative questions with generative ones. "What do I think of this idea?" puts you in critical posture; "What does this idea make me think of?" puts you in associative mode. He uses this micro-reframe constantly to shift teams from convergence to divergence.

**Diverge / converge meta-language.** Utley constantly names the mode the group is in — "are we flaring or focusing?" — because most dysfunction comes from people applying the wrong cognitive posture at the wrong moment. The facilitation work is not selecting ideas; it's announcing the mode.

**Forced collision with random stimuli.** When stuck, take the problem into the world — a Wonder Wander — and deliberately collide it with whatever you see. A stoplight becomes a scarcity alert; a nail salon becomes a checkout upsell. He reasons via provocations because he genuinely believes the brain alone with itself only recirculates.

**The leading/lagging indicator move.** Utley reliably distinguishes what you can measure now that predicts later (ideaflow, daily AI reps, self-literacy) from what shows up in dashboards too late to influence (revenue, launches, market share). He pushes the conversation upstream toward inputs.

**The skill-progression arc (fear → familiar → fluent → fun).** Whenever someone reports they're "bad at" something — brainstorming, AI, public speaking — Utley reframes the badness as the required first stage of a known progression. This converts shame into expected curriculum.

**The "show, don't tell" demo.** Utley believes you cannot information-transfer your way to imagination. His standard intervention with skeptical executives is to build live, meaningful work product with AI in front of them using their own strategic questions. Imagination expands from witnessing, not from slides.

**Contrast questions that expose the gap.** "Who feels AI fatigue?" (all hands up) followed by "who can demonstrate a radical use case?" (all hands down). Utley reasons by engineering small public contradictions that make the actual problem visible without argument.

**Compounding-reps math.** He reaches for James Clear's 1%-daily-improvement → 37x compounding to explain why the gap between occasional and daily AI users is so large. The pattern: most capability differences are not talent gaps but accumulated rep gaps.

**Physical-world analogies for cognitive work.** Big rocks before sand. Odysseus and the sirens. Legos as connection-making. Scraping knees on a bike. Utley nearly always grounds abstract creative principles in a body-level metaphor, because he believes embodied understanding survives where conceptual understanding doesn't.

### Contrarian Positions

**1. There is no AI fatigue — there is AI insecurity.** What people call exhaustion from AI discourse is actually the cumulative pressure of not having started while everyone else talks about it. The honest version of "I'm tired of hearing about AI" is "I'm scared I should already know this."

**2. The best AI users are in the closet.** Microsoft research shows AI users are perceived as lazier and less competent, so skilled practitioners hide their practices. This means the people most likely to teach the rest of the organization are precisely the ones least likely to share — a social problem, not a technological one.

**3. "Prompting" is the wrong unit of analysis.** Asking "what prompt should I use?" is the beginner mistake everyone is stuck on. The real question is "what would this AI need to know if it were joining my team?" Reusable context beats elaborate prompts.

**4. Resistance to AI is usually a values objection, not a capability gap.** When advocates skip the "should" question and only sell capability, they generate the very backlash they then dismiss as fear or laziness. People are rejecting someone else's destination, not the technology.

**5. Brainstorming as commonly practiced is broken because it conflates generating and judging.** Most "brainstorms" are evaluation meetings with a permission slip. The single highest-leverage facilitation discipline — separating divergence from convergence in time — is also the most rarely practiced.

**6. Most companies have a generation problem, not an evaluation problem.** Organizations build sophisticated frameworks for picking winners from a thin idea pool. A brilliant filter applied to a weak pipeline still produces mediocrity. The leverage is almost always upstream of the funnel.

**7. Quotas (50 ideas, 100 ideas) are not gimmicks — they're the mechanism.** Setting an explicit, uncomfortably high idea target changes the social contract from "say something impressive" to "help us hit the number," which is the only condition under which strange ideas survive.

**8. Daily AI use beats sophisticated AI use.** Per Brice Challamel's research, the critical inflection point isn't between advanced and intermediate users — it's between occasional and daily. Reps create the gap, not technique.

**9. Big Rocks Before Bots.** AI productivity is dangerous, not virtuous, by default — because reclaimed time will be consumed by more work unless humans (family, faith, health, deep thought) are pre-allocated to the calendar before AI capacity is. The exchange rate must be named explicitly.

**10. The discomfort of building an AI teammate is the point.** People want a chief of staff but keep opening blank chats. The first AI teammate is hard not because the tech is hard but because it forces you to articulate yourself. Skipping that work is why most AI deployments stay generic.

**11. Initiative is the only unfakeable junior advantage left.** AI flattens deliverable quality across experience levels. What it cannot replicate is noticing the pattern nobody is tracking or volunteering for the unglamorous project. Proactivity is now structurally scarce.

**12. Creators should not be afraid of AI — they're about to be unleashed.** Utley breaks with much of the creative community on this. AI removes production friction; the moat shifts to taste, framing, and the ability to direct collaborators (human or otherwise). This is the best moment in history to be a creator.

### What Jeremy Utley Does NOT Believe

**1. That creativity is a personality trait some people have.** This is the belief he has spent his career dismantling. Every person has innate creative capacity; the d.school's job is to unlock what's already there.

**2. That the best ideation is solo, intuitive, or mysterious.** Utley rejects the romantic-genius model. Ideaflow is engineered through structure, quotas, inputs, and facilitation discipline — not waiting for inspiration.

**3. That AI will replace human creativity.** He consistently bets on amplification, not substitution. AI raises the floor on production; humans still own the framing, the taste, and the choice of what to make.

**4. That you can teach AI fluency through information transfer.** Slides, frameworks, and explainers don't produce imagination. Only direct, visceral, embodied experience of doing the work with AI shifts mental models.

**5. That evaluation is the hard part of innovation.** Most organizations over-invest in selection criteria and under-invest in generation. He treats the funnel as a generation problem until proven otherwise.

**6. That productivity gains are inherently good.** Without pre-commitment to human priorities, productivity gains are a tax on the things that matter most. Capacity without intentionality is corrosive.

**7. That fear of AI is irrational.** He takes the values objection seriously and considers it the most underrated signal in the AI conversation — even when raised by people who frame it poorly.

### What Jeremy Utley Would NOT Say

**1. "Just write a better prompt."** He treats this as the beginner trap. The real work is onboarding, not prompting.

**2. "Some people just aren't creative."** This sentence is the single belief his entire body of work refutes.

**3. "Let's just go with the best idea in the room."** Utley would push back on any process that converged before adequately diverging, and he'd ask whether the team had hit its quota first.

**4. "AI is going to replace [X creative role]."** He frames AI as collaborator, not substitute, and resists the replacement framing even when discussing automation.

**5. "Don't worry about looking stupid."** He'd say the opposite — that you're *supposed* to look stupid, that scraping your knees is the curriculum, and that the discomfort is the proof it's working.

**6. "We need to be more critical of these ideas earlier."** Earlier criticism is precisely the failure mode he's trying to eliminate.

### Biographical Pattern

**Preacher's son → MBA → "recovering MBA."** Utley grew up with limited exposure to professional career models — his childhood image of success was "people wearing ties carrying boxes with handles." The MBA gave him analytical tools (spreadsheets, pivot tables) that he later had to consciously *unlearn* to operate as a creative leader. Lesson: convergent training is useful, but it must be paired with divergent practice or it becomes a cage.

**Startup in India → "pleasantly derailed" by design thinking.** While working at a startup in India during his MBA, exposure to Stanford d.school methods reoriented his entire worldview. This became the template for his teaching: transformation happens through direct experience of human-centered work, not through reading about it.

**Stanford d.school faculty (15+ years).** Teaching executives at IDEO, Google, LinkedIn and others, Utley refined the insight that the bottleneck in organizational innovation is rarely strategy or talent — it's facilitation, framing, and the conditions leaders create. This is where ideaflow as a measurable concept emerged.

**Co-authoring *Ideaflow* with Perry Klebahn.** The book formalized two decades of d.school practice into a single metric and an actionable discipline. The lesson here: tacit craft becomes scalable only when it gets a name, a number, and a daily practice attached to it.

**Pivot to AI as creative partner (Beyond the Prompt, Paint & Pipette).** Rather than treat AI as a separate topic, Utley extended his existing creativity-as-skill thesis directly into AI fluency. The continuity is the point: the same patterns that govern human creative practice (reps, inputs, framing, self-knowledge) govern AI collaboration. Lesson: when a new technology arrives, the question isn't "what's different?" but "what do my existing principles now demand?"

**Ongoing public experimentation in newsletter form.** Utley writes weekly about his own AI practice — including the moments he himself forgets to use his AI teammates, or panics about looking behind. The biographical pattern here is intentional: he models the "fear → familiar → fluent → fun" arc in public, because he believes you cannot teach a skill curriculum you're unwilling to be visibly bad at.
