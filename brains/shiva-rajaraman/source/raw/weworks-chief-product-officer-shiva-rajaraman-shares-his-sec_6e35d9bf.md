# What Are the Ingredients of a Successful Advisor Relationship? WeWork's New CPO Gives His Advice

By [Heather Hartnett](https://www.forbes.com/sites/heatherhartnett/ "https://www.forbes.com/sites/heatherhartnett/"),

Former Contributor.

Aug 24, 2017, 10:54am EDTAug 24, 2017, 01:13pm EDT

This article is more than8years old.

Founders often want an advisor, but the rules of the road aren’t always black and white. So what are the keys to making this relationship work? “It’s about communication, having clear goals, asking the right questions, and a sense of humor,” says Shiva Rajaraman, [WeWork’s new Chief Product Officer](https://therealdeal.com/2017/08/17/wework-hires-apple-exec-to-manage-software/). Arguably, one of the best product leaders in the business, Shiva somehow finds time to be a valuable advisor to many startups, (including [Clark](https://hiclark.com/), a Human Ventures portfolio company), [Look](http://www.joinlook.com/), [Cooler](https://cooler.tv/), and [WhizKids](http://www.whizkidzmedia.com/) Media. In the first of my two part post on advisors, Shiva shares his insight for founders to strike the right chemistry with an advisor.

**_Heather Hartnett: How can founders start to identify which advisor would be a good fit?_**

**Shiva Rajaraman:** First spend some time identifying where you need help. This feels obvious but I’ve often seen individuals lack precision on what they need and as a result some relationships start rushed and end sloppy. There’s this saying by Confucius, “Real knowledge is knowing the extent of your ignorance.” I know it’s a cliche, but I pretty much live my life trying to stay honest about that when it comes to building teams or seeking mentors. I think it’s the best lens through which to craft an advising relationship. Maybe you are scaling your team for the first time; maybe you want a trusted sounding board as you craft your message or presence; or perhaps you need specific guidance to grow your business whether it be funnel optimization or product differentiation. The more you can hone in here the better as it will help you both identify the right person(s) but also shed light on the right commitment and cadence required.

Then, start to seek advisors with expertise in one or more of these areas and begin a dialogue with them to understand whether the challenges excite them and what kind of commitment you and they can invest to make it happen.

**_HH: Once a founder has identified a good fit, how do you recommend they start that relationship?_**

SR: Break bread. Any advising relationship needs to start with some level of chemistry. I don’t think this is about finding your soulmate, but it’s helpful if you minimally share a common passion and certainly a sense of humor. During those meetings, it’s good on both sides to get a sense of how people react when challenged: How quickly can you springboard off of each other’s topics?; how clearly can you educate one another? I, for one, have a communication style that features an overly ornate choice of words and can manifest itself in a shotgun spray of ideas. If the person I work with doesn’t check that now and then or doesn’t like to interject or riff, I can see how it can be an problem. It’s so important to get this right.

**HH: You are an advisor to several companies and I have seen first hand how effective you are with your time. How do you recommend setting up the relationship to maximize the benefit for both the company and the advisor?**

SR: You need to develop a rhythm. Perhaps it’s weekly or monthly syncs but make sure you establish and stick to it. As the company (or advisor) identifies opportunities or challenges, there’s an established geometry you can count on to assemble thoughts, discuss them, and return with solutions or suggestions.

I also think it’s important to establish whether “off cycle” is encouraged or not. Quick calls or texts? Emails on very specific topics or thoughts?  In some of the advising work I’ve done, I’ve found that the role I play is more of a coach for a series of small items. What works for me is being available by text or email anytime but with the expectation that it’s quick feedback. And when you play the role of a coach you want to offer continuous feedback on small things so they add up and you become at ease with giving and receiving feedback. No one improves if their tennis coach sees them once a quarter. But this may not make sense for many advising relationships—tie the engagement cadence and style to your goal.

**_HH: What’s one mistake founders often make when communicating with advisors?_**

SR: While most advisors probably will give you feedback on everything from UX to aesthetics and even whether your logo is awesome or not, when it comes to substance, avoid an overreliance on any one person for this type of feedback. There are better ways to gather user research. Instead, I would ask your advisors to keep you alert to dark areas you may be missing. Ask these types of questions: Can you suggest options or tactics we haven’t considered? We are very biased toward a specific audience; can you suggest ways to reach new groups or ensure we don’t unconsciously end up in a bubble that will hurt growth later? Can you suggest metrics that we should measure or how best to ensure our current metrics don’t lead us into short-term sandpit? Given what you know about us what future capabilities should we be investing in now? What can wait?

**_HH: On the flip side, if you have been approached to be an advisor by a founder and you believe you can add a lot of value, what are the right questions you should be asking of the founder/company before taking on the responsibility?_**

SR: Assuming you are intrigued by the mission and the person, I think it is good to have a clear picture of what success looks like. Is the person looking for a trusted peer they can learn from and with whom they can sharpen their insights? Or are they looking for a specific outcome and deliverable, perhaps even a capability they want their team to ultimate own and drive independently? It’s good to really understand that so you can lead toward the “the exit” if need be and/or angle your energy to the right goals at the right time.

I also try to understand who will be involved from the company, what are their expectations and/or sensitivities, and what the company believes are core tenets of how they work so you can ensure you are a good fit and mirror existing culture.

**_HH: How can you ensure that the relationship stays strong?_**

SR: Anticipate that you may want to sanity check the relationship 6 months in, if not sooner. While people typically set up an initial term often of 1 year to start, go ahead and set up some wellness checks along the way so you can tweak the relationship and steer it in the right direction.

And remember, the best advising relationships are those that challenge you. You don’t want someone that panders to your ego and makes you feel good all day, but rather someone who is 100 percent impassioned to your improvement even if it feels like a penance. These people help you make the toughest people decisions, push you to invest in the capabilities your company will be known for decades from now, and even tell you when your speech is tiring and your jokes are weak. Remind your advisors to always be honest and never pull punches. You can always hug it out later.
